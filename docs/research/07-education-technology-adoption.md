## Theoretical Background

### 1. Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) — Davis (1989)
**Core content:** TAM posits that perceived usefulness and perceived ease of use are the primary determinants of behavioral intention to use a technology, and that intention predicts actual use. The model was extended (TAM2, UTAUT) to include social influence, facilitating conditions, and individual characteristics including education.
**Relevance to this study:** TAM provides the individual-level psychological framework for understanding why nurses with different educational backgrounds differ in technology adoption. Higher-educated nurses may perceive greater usefulness because their training explicitly integrates clinical informatics, raising both perceived competence and intention to use. Multiple studies in this review applied TAM directly to nurses (Alrashedi et al., 2026; Walzer et al., 2025).

### 2. DigComp Framework — European Commission (Vuorikari et al., 2022)
**Core content:** The European Digital Competence Framework defines five competence areas (information and data literacy, communication and collaboration, digital content creation, safety, and problem-solving) and four proficiency levels (foundational, intermediate, advanced, highly specialised). It underpins national digital skills policies across EU member states.
**Relevance to this study:** DigComp provides the measurement framework used by several European nursing studies. The framework explicitly maps competence acquisition to formal education exposure, supporting the hypothesis that higher-educated nurses should score at higher proficiency levels.

### 3. TIGER Nursing Informatics Competency Framework — TIGER Initiative (2017)
**Core content:** Technology Informatics Guiding Education Reform defines three domains for all nurses — basic computer skills, information literacy, and clinical information management — and delineates how nursing education at each level (associate, bachelor's, master's) should develop these competencies progressively.
**Relevance to this study:** TIGER is the dominant instrument used in nursing informatics competency surveys (Hariyati et al., 2024). It operationalises the idea that educational credential level corresponds to progressively deeper technology integration skills.

### 4. Human Capital Theory — Becker (1964), adapted to digital health
**Core content:** Investment in formal education increases worker productivity and capability. In health informatics literature, this translates to the argument that more years of formal education expose nurses to structured, theory-grounded technology curricula and critical appraisal skills that increase adoption of evidence-based technologies.
**Relevance to this study:** The human capital perspective predicts a monotonic, positive relationship between years of formal education and digital technology use — directly congruent with H2.

This is a crucial methodological caveat for the thesis study design and analysis.

## Research Gap Summary

**Gap 1 — Absence of Slovak-specific clinical nursing data:** No published study measures technology use frequency by education level specifically among Slovak clinical nurses (hospital bedside nurses, community nurses). The Pajari et al. (2025) Slovak subsample covers only nurse educators. The thesis directly fills this gap.

**Gap 2 — Cross-credential threshold comparisons are underrepresented:** Most studies compare within a credential tier (e.g., 3rd vs. 4th year undergraduates) or sample homogeneously from one degree level. Cross-sectional studies comparing secondary vocational nursing graduates against bachelor's-level and specialist/master's-level nurses in the same institutional context are scarce. This is the exact design tested by H2.

**Gap 3 — Technology use frequency is conflated with competence:** Existing literature disproportionately measures digital competence (knowledge and skills) rather than frequency of actual technology use in clinical practice. The Comparcini et al. (2025) study is among the few to measure use frequency directly. The thesis should operationalise both constructs independently.

**Gap 4 — CEE healthcare infrastructure and digitalization context is unexplored:** Most studies come from Finland, Nordic countries, Anglo-Saxon contexts, and East Asia. CEE healthcare systems are underdigitalized relative to Western Europe (OECD Health at a Glance Europe 2024), and the interaction between healthcare system digitalization maturity and nurse education level on technology use has not been studied in this region.

**Gap 5 — Interaction effects of education level, age, and work experience:** The Li et al. (2025) study found that age and experience may override education level when the education range is narrow. No study in the 2021–2026 period has modelled the interaction between education level, years of clinical experience, and age simultaneously on technology use frequency among practising nurses in a CEE context.

## Proposed Theoretical Framework

The study adopts an integrated framework combining TAM (perceived usefulness and ease of use as proximate determinants of technology adoption intention and use frequency) with Human Capital Theory (formal education as a distal determinant that shapes both perceived competence and exposure to informatics curricula). The TIGER competency framework grounds the measurement of nursing-specific technology competence, and DigComp provides the language for categorising proficiency levels.

The proposed causal pathway for H2 is:

**Education level (secondary < bachelor's < specialization/master's)** → **Informatics curriculum exposure and structured digital training during qualification** → **Higher perceived digital competence and perceived usefulness of clinical technologies** → **Greater frequency of technology use in clinical nursing practice**

This pathway is moderated by: (a) age and years of experience, (b) hospital/institutional digitalization environment, and (c) individual technology attitudes. The thesis survey should collect data on all three moderators to allow subgroup analysis and to isolate the education effect from confounders — this responds directly to the null finding of Li et al. (2025) and the interaction issue identified in Walzer et al. (2025).
