## Theoretical Background

### Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) — Davis, 1989

**Originator:** Fred D. Davis (1989)

**Core content:** TAM posits that two cognitive beliefs — perceived usefulness and perceived ease of use — are the primary determinants of an individual's behavioral intention to use a technology, which in turn predicts actual use. The model is the most widely applied framework in health informatics research.

**Relevance to this study:** Multiple primary studies in this review apply TAM or its extensions to explain variance in nursing staff's technology adoption. Perceived ease of use is repeatedly found to be moderated by years of experience and prior computer training, making TAM the dominant lens for interpreting H3.

---

### Unified Theory of Acceptance and Use of Technology (UTAUT) — Venkatesh et al., 2003

**Originator:** Viswanath Venkatesh, Michael G. Morris, Gordon B. Davis, and Fred D. Davis (2003)

**Core content:** UTAUT integrates eight predecessor models into four core constructs (performance expectancy, effort expectancy, social influence, facilitating conditions) and explicitly includes age, gender, experience, and voluntariness of use as moderating variables. It explains up to 70% of variance in use intention.

**Relevance to this study:** The model formally encodes "experience" as a theoretically expected moderator. Studies in this review that explicitly test UTAUT (Klawunn et al., 2024; Rodríguez-Picazo et al., 2021) provide the strongest theoretical justification for H3. Specifically, UTAUT predicts that effort expectancy has a stronger negative effect on older and more experienced workers.

---

### FITT Framework — Ammenwerth et al., 2006

**Originator:** Elske Ammenwerth et al. (2006)

**Core content:** The Fit between Individuals, Task, and Technology (FITT) framework holds that successful technology adoption depends on the alignment between individual characteristics (including experience, skill, attitude), the task being performed, and the technology's properties.

**Relevance to this study:** De Leeuw et al. (2020), whose findings remain the only in-depth study specifically recruiting self-identified digitally lagging experienced nurses (mean tenure 28.9 years), applies this framework to explain how long clinical experience, when not accompanied by concurrent digital skill development, creates a negative person-technology fit that generates avoidance behavior. This directly informs the mechanism proposed for H3.

---

### Generational Cohort Theory — Strauss and Howe, 1991; applied to nursing by Mellum, 2024

**Originators:** William Strauss and Neil Howe (1991)

**Core content:** Cohort theory posits that individuals shaped by the same sociohistorical period share characteristic values, work styles, and technological orientations. In nursing, four cohorts are currently active: Baby Boomers, Generation X, Millennials, and Generation Z.

**Relevance to this study:** Years of clinical experience is structurally correlated with generational cohort membership. Nurses with 20+ years of experience are predominantly Baby Boomers or early Gen X who received foundational education without digital tools, providing a structural explanation for why experience may negatively predict technology attitudes independent of individual preference. Mellum (2024) cautions against deterministic stereotyping, which the study's quantitative design can address by testing actual correlations rather than assumed ones.

## Research Gap Summary

**Gap 1 — Direct operationalization of "years of experience" as a predictor:** Most quantitative studies include years of experience as a control variable rather than a primary predictor, or collapse it into broad categories. No study in this review was specifically designed to test whether clinical tenure is a statistically significant predictor of technology attitude scores while controlling for digital competence, anxiety, and organizational support simultaneously. This master's thesis fills this gap directly.

**Gap 2 — European (Central/Eastern) nursing context:** The identified empirical studies originate primarily from South Korea, Saudi Arabia, China, Taiwan, Finland, Germany, South Africa, the Netherlands, and the UK. The Slovak nursing context — characterized by a historically centralized healthcare system with late-stage EMR adoption, staffing shortages, and specific generational training backgrounds — is entirely absent from the literature. The transferability of findings from East Asian or Gulf hospital contexts to a Central European nursing workforce is limited.

**Gap 3 — Distinction between age and years of experience:** The literature frequently conflates these two constructs. Older nurses may have more years of experience, but the independent contributions of age (generational digital socialization) versus tenure (accumulated practice context) are not separated. This thesis, by collecting both variables and examining years of experience as the primary predictor of H3, provides a methodological refinement over prior research.

**Gap 4 — Non-linear and threshold effects:** None of the identified studies test whether the experience-attitude relationship is linear or whether threshold effects exist (e.g., a curvilinear relationship where mid-career nurses show the lowest acceptance due to highest workload, while very experienced nurses show higher acceptance due to established routines). The dataset collected for this thesis permits such analysis.

**Gap 5 — Mechanism versus association:** The qualitative literature has characterized the mechanism (training-era socialization, negative experience accumulation, self-protective avoidance), but quantitative studies rarely include mediating variables that would allow causal pathway testing. Future research building on this thesis should include digital self-efficacy as a mediator between years of experience and attitude.

## Proposed Theoretical Framework

The study adopts a modified TAM/UTAUT perspective integrating generational cohort theory and the FITT individual-fit dimension. The proposed framework positions **years of clinical experience** as a distal predictor of digital technology attitudes, operating through two primary mechanisms:

1. **Training-era socialization pathway:** Nurses trained in paper-based environments develop professional identity and clinical routines that are cognitively inconsistent with digital workflow requirements. Longer experience corresponds to more deeply entrenched paper-era schemas.

2. **Negative experience accumulation pathway:** Each failed or unsupported encounter with digital technology incrementally increases information anxiety and reduces digital self-efficacy, producing avoidance behavior that further delays skill development.

Both pathways are moderated by **organizational culture** (leadership digital climate, availability of structured training, peer support structures) and **individual digital competence** (prior technology exposure, computer skills developed outside nursing education).

The framework predicts a significant negative association between years of experience and technology attitude scores (H3), with the effect size moderated downward in institutions with supportive digital cultures and upward in institutions with poor implementation support. Controlling for organizational culture variables in the regression model will allow isolation of the net experience effect.

## Handoff to Methodology Expert

The literature confirms that years of clinical experience is a theoretically grounded predictor of technology attitudes, operating through generational socialization and negative experience accumulation pathways. Key methodological notes:

- A **cross-sectional quantitative design** with a structured questionnaire is appropriate and consistent with the majority of comparable studies. The NATC, HPHTAAS, or adapted TAM-based attitude scales used in Alrasheeday et al. (2023) and Güllü & Aytekin Kanadli (2025) are the most directly comparable instruments.
- **Multivariate regression** (not bivariate correlation alone) is required to isolate the experience effect while controlling for age, education level, prior computer training, and organizational support — all of which have independent evidence of association with attitude scores.
- **Sample stratification** by tenure categories (0-5, 6-10, 11-20, 21+ years) enables detection of threshold effects; minimum n per cell should be 30 for parametric analysis.
- The null finding in the Turkish ICU study (Güllü & Aytekin Kanadli, 2025) and positive finding in the Saudi study (Alrasheeday et al., 2023) suggest power and setting specificity must be addressed in the limitations section.

## Handoff to Writing Coach

The literature review establishes four arguments that should structure the theoretical chapter of the master's thesis:

1. TAM and UTAUT explicitly model experience as a moderating variable — this is the formal theoretical justification for H3.
2. Generational cohort theory provides the historical mechanism: nurses trained pre-digitalization face schema-incompatibility challenges.
3. Qualitative evidence (De Leeuw et al.; Dowding et al.; Mabaso et al.) provides rich illustrative material for why experienced nurses resist, which can frame the quantitative findings in the discussion chapter.
4. The evidence for organizational culture as a moderator means the discussion must contextualize any finding about experience within the institutional setting of Slovak hospitals — a point that both strengthens originality and appropriately limits generalizability claims.
