## Theoretical Background

### 1. Donabedian's Structure-Process-Outcome Model (SPO)
- **Originator:** Avedis Donabedian (1966, foundational; continuously applied)
- **Core content:** Healthcare quality is assessable through three interconnected domains: structure (organizational and technological resources), process (clinical activities performed), and outcome (patient health results). Digital tools operate at the structural level but propagate effects through clinical processes to measurable patient outcomes.
- **Relevance to this study:** Provides the analytical scaffold for H4. EHR systems constitute structural elements; nursing documentation and handover represent process variables; medication error rates, falls, and patient satisfaction constitute outcomes. This model justifies why nurses' perception of EHR quality improvement is an intermediate rather than terminal indicator.

### 2. Sociotechnical Systems Theory
- **Originator:** Trist and Bamforth (1951); applied to health informatics by Sittig and Singh (2010)
- **Core content:** Technology cannot be understood in isolation from the social, organizational, and workflow contexts in which it is deployed. Successful digitalization requires alignment between the technical system (EHR design, interoperability) and the social system (nursing roles, training, culture).
- **Relevance to this study:** Explains why EHR adoption alone does not guarantee quality improvement (von Wedel et al., 2022); user-perceived usefulness and usability mediate outcomes. This directly contextualizes H4 — nurse perception is not merely subjective but is a sociotechnically valid measure of system effectiveness.

### 3. Technology Acceptance Model (TAM)
- **Originator:** Fred Davis (1989)
- **Core content:** Perceived usefulness and perceived ease of use are the primary determinants of behavioral intention to use a technology.
- **Relevance to this study:** Predicts variance in nurses' self-reported EHR quality ratings. Nurses who receive adequate training and encounter user-friendly interfaces are more likely to perceive EHR as quality-improving, which is precisely what H4 tests empirically.

### 4. Nursing Minimum Data Set and Nursing-Sensitive Indicators Framework
- **Originators:** Werley and Lang (1988); ANA NDNQI programme (1998-)
- **Core content:** Specific patient outcomes are directly attributable to nursing care volume, skill mix, and practice quality. Key nursing-sensitive indicators include: pressure injuries, patient falls, medication errors, healthcare-associated infections, failure to rescue, and patient satisfaction.
- **Relevance to this study:** Defines the outcome endpoints through which digital tool impact on nursing care quality is measured. EHR-mediated documentation of NSIs enables automated, real-time quality surveillance (von Gerich et al., 2022).

## Research Gap Summary

1. **Gap 1 — Slovak-specific evidence is absent.** No study in the reviewed literature reports on Slovak nurses' perceptions of EHR quality impact. The single study providing a Slovak data point (Pajari et al., 2025) addressed nurse educators' self-assessed digital competence, not clinical nurses' perceptions of EHR's effect on care quality. H4 fills this gap directly.

2. **Gap 2 — Positive versus burden dimensions of EHR are rarely disentangled.** Most surveys capture aggregate satisfaction. Studies show nurses can simultaneously perceive EHR as improving quality (medication safety, data completeness) while experiencing it as a workload burden (Jacques et al., 2025; Canfell et al., 2024). Research isolating which specific EHR functions nurses associate with quality improvement — rather than measuring overall EHR sentiment — is rare and methodologically important.

3. **Gap 3 — Central and Eastern European (CEE) implementation context is underrepresented.** The majority of digitalized hospital studies originate from Germany, the United States, the United Kingdom, Australia, and East Asia. CEE countries, including Slovakia, operate under different healthcare financing structures, infrastructure maturity levels, and nursing culture contexts. Slovakia is among the EU member states with lowest eHealth adoption rates, making findings from Nordic or Anglophone contexts only partially transferable.

4. **Gap 4 — Longitudinal follow-up on nursing quality outcomes post-EHR implementation is scarce.** Most studies are cross-sectional (von Wedel et al., 2022; Campione & Liu, 2024) or short quasi-experimental (Tataei et al., 2023). The medium-term trajectory of nurses' quality perceptions as EHR systems mature post-implementation is not established.

5. **Gap 5 — Nursing-sensitive outcome measurement from EHR data remains in infancy.** Von Gerich et al. (2022) demonstrated that 75% of quality-relevant expressions in EHRs reside in unstructured free text. Without NLP infrastructure, automated quality monitoring of nursing-sensitive indicators is not operationalized in most hospitals, including Slovak facilities.

## Proposed Theoretical Framework

The study employs a **Sociotechnical Quality Model** integrating three theoretical layers:

**Layer 1 — Structural (Donabedian):** EHR infrastructure, interoperability, clinical decision support configuration, and nursing workstation design constitute the structural preconditions for quality improvement.

**Layer 2 — Processual (TAM + Sociotechnical Theory):** Nurses' perceived usefulness and ease of use of EHR determine their engagement with the system. Training adequacy, leadership digital competence, and organizational culture mediate this layer. This is where H4 operates — nurses who rate EHR as improving quality are registering their experienced value of the process layer.

**Layer 3 — Outcome (NDNQI/Nursing Sensitive Indicators):** Medication error rates, patient falls, pressure injuries, handover information completeness, and patient satisfaction constitute the measurable nursing-sensitive outcomes that digitalization aims to improve.

**Conceptual model:**

```
[EHR/Digital Tool Adoption] 
        |
        v
[Structural Configuration: interoperability, CDSS, training]
        |
        v (moderated by: usability, digital competence, leadership)
[Process Change: documentation quality, handover completeness, communication]
        |
        v
[Nurse Perception Layer: perceived quality/safety improvement ← H4 target]
        |
        v
[Nursing-Sensitive Outcomes: medication errors, falls, HAI, patient satisfaction]
```

H4 is positioned between the process and outcome layers: nurses' rating of EHR as improving quality is both an intermediate outcome and a sociotechnically valid predictor of objective quality metrics.

## Handoff to Methodology Expert

The literature review establishes the following methodological intelligence for study design:

- **Dominant methodology in the field:** Cross-sectional surveys (most common), systematic reviews (highest citation impact), quasi-experimental pre-post designs (used for handover and CDSS studies). A cross-sectional self-administered questionnaire design is aligned with the dominant methodological tradition for H4 testing.
- **Measurement instruments used:** KLAS EHR satisfaction scales; NDNQI databases; vendor-provided time-tracking data; custom Likert scales measuring perceived EHR usefulness. The study should use a validated nurse perception of EHR quality scale, or adapt established instruments (e.g., KLAS Global EHR Satisfaction dimensions) to the Slovak context.
- **Sample size benchmarks:** Studies range from n=20 (qualitative saturation) to n=8,880 (cross-sectional survey). For a master thesis establishing H4 with 80% power at α=0.05 to detect a >50% proportion, a minimum of 97 nurses is required (one-sample binomial test); recommend targeting 150-200 respondents to account for non-response and subgroup analysis.
- **Key confounders to control:** Hospital digitization maturity level, nursing department type (ICU vs. general ward), nurse tenure, training received on EHR, and hospital size.
- **Theoretical precedent for H4 threshold:** Von Wedel et al. (2022) found user-perceived EHR value to be the operative variable in outcome improvement. If <50% of nurses perceive EHR as quality-improving, H4 rejection would indicate a Slovak digitalization implementation deficit rather than a technology failure.
