## Theoretical Background

### Theory 1: Technology Acceptance Model (TAM)
- **Originator**: Davis, F.D. (1989). *Perceived usefulness, perceived ease of use, and user acceptance of information technology. MIS Quarterly, 13*(3), 319–340.
- **Core content**: TAM proposes that user adoption of any information technology is primarily determined by two perceptual constructs: Perceived Usefulness (PU — the degree to which a person believes the technology will enhance job performance) and Perceived Ease of Use (PEOU — the degree to which a person believes using the technology will be free of effort). These perceptions shape attitude toward use, which in turn determines behavioral intention, which predicts actual use behavior.
- **Relevance to this study**: TAM is the dominant theoretical lens in nursing digital technology research. It maps directly onto H1 (technology use → efficiency: operationalizable as PU) and H3 (experience → attitudes: experienced nurses may develop different PU/PEOU appraisals). Multiple included studies use TAM-derived instruments (perceived usefulness scales, ease-of-use subscales) as their primary outcome measures.

### Theory 2: Unified Theory of Acceptance and Use of Technology (UTAUT)
- **Originator**: Venkatesh, V., Morris, M.G., Davis, G.B., & Davis, F.D. (2003). *User acceptance of information technology: Toward a unified view. MIS Quarterly, 27*(3), 425–478.
- **Core content**: UTAUT integrates eight prior technology acceptance models into four core determinants of behavioral intention: Performance Expectancy (PE), Effort Expectancy (EE), Social Influence (SI), and Facilitating Conditions (FC). It adds moderating roles for gender, age, experience, and voluntariness of use.
- **Relevance to this study**: UTAUT's explicit moderating variables (age, experience, education level) directly operationalize the hypotheses H2 and H3. Performance Expectancy aligns with efficiency perceptions (H1). Han et al. (2024) and the Korea-based study used UTAUT constructs in a nursing digital health context, finding PE and FC as the strongest predictors of nurses' adoption intentions.

### Theory 3: NASSS Framework (Nonadoption, Abandonment, Scale-up, Spread, and Sustainability)
- **Originator**: Greenhalgh, T., Wherton, J., Papoutsi, C., et al. (2017). *Beyond adoption: A new framework for theorizing and evaluating nonadoption, abandonment, and challenges to the scale-up, spread, and sustainability of health and care technologies. Journal of Medical Internet Research, 19*(11), e367.
- **Core content**: NASSS goes beyond individual attitude to address why technologies are not adopted or are abandoned even after initial uptake, identifying multiple interacting domains: the condition, the technology, the value proposition, the adopter system, the organization, the wider context, and the embeddedness over time.
- **Relevance to this study**: Applied in the Walzer et al. (2025) umbrella review to map 52 distinct factors across six domains. It contextualizes why positive attitudes alone do not guarantee adoption, which is directly relevant to the gap between nurses' stated intentions and actual use behaviors — a tension documented in the Shanghai cohort (Xie et al., 2025).

Organizational context (leadership, infrastructure, culture) consistently mediates whether positive attitudes translate into actual technology use.

## Research Gap Summary

1. **Absence of Slovak clinical nurse data**: The only study with a Slovak nursing population (Pajari et al., 2025) was limited to nurse educators in academic settings (N=290, 25% response rate). No published peer-reviewed study examines attitudes, perceptions, or technology acceptance among bedside clinical nurses in Slovakia. This is the primary gap that the proposed thesis addresses and its primary justification for originality.

2. **CEE-specific attitude measurement**: Validated attitude instruments (ITASH, UTAUT questionnaire, GAAIS) have not been psychometrically validated in Slovak or broader CEE clinical nursing populations. The use of unvalidated proprietary tools (as in Bartosiewicz et al., 2021) or tools validated only in Western European or non-European contexts limits the transferability of findings. The thesis must either validate an existing instrument or adapt and test one for Slovak use.

3. **Intention–behavior gap unexplored in European nursing**: The documented gap between stated adoption intention and actual technology use behavior (Xie et al., 2025: 78.9% intention vs. 44.9% actual frequent use) has not been investigated in European nursing contexts. European studies predominantly measure attitudes and intentions; few track actual use behaviors longitudinally.

4. **Conflicting age-effect evidence**: Most studies confirm an age-related decline in digital technology attitudes and competence (Bartosiewicz et al., 2021; Girasek et al., 2024; El Arab et al., 2025), but Traulsen et al. (2024) found no significant age-attitude correlation in a German nursing home context. The conditions under which age predicts attitudes — and whether this is confounded by training access, technology familiarity, or occupational generation effects — remain theoretically unresolved and empirically testable in a Slovak sample (H3).

5. **Training as an unmeasured moderator**: While training is consistently identified as the strongest facilitator of adoption (Nascimento et al., 2023; Walzer et al., 2025), few studies include training quality, duration, and recency as independent variables in attitude regression models. Slovak nurses' access to digital technology training is not documented in the literature, creating an opportunity to test H2 with greater precision than most prior studies.

6. **Technostress–attitude relationship understudied**: Two German studies document the burnout consequences of technostress, but technostress as a mediator or moderator of technology acceptance has not been integrated into TAM/UTAUT models in nursing research. This represents a methodological opportunity for the proposed thesis.

## Proposed Theoretical Framework

The proposed thesis integrates TAM and UTAUT constructs within a CEE-specific contextual model:

**Level 1 — Individual characteristics** (independent variables): Age, years of clinical experience, highest education level attained, previous digital technology training (type, duration, recency), self-assessed digital competence.

**Level 2 — Attitude constructs** (mediating variables): Perceived Usefulness (PU), Perceived Ease of Use (PEOU), Technostress indicators (overload, complexity, unreliability), Digital self-efficacy.

**Level 3 — Adoption outcomes** (dependent variables): Attitude toward digital technology use, behavioral intention to use, actual use frequency.

**Level 4 — Organizational context** (moderating conditions): Leadership support, IT infrastructure quality, availability of training, participation in technology design.

This framework predicts: H1 → PU mediates the technology use–efficiency attitude relationship; H2 → education level and training access predict PU, PEOU, and self-efficacy; H3 → age and experience predict PU and PEOU, moderated by organizational context.

## Handoff to Methodology Expert

The literature supports a quantitative cross-sectional survey design as the primary methodology, consistent with 75%+ of studies in this field. The following design decisions are evidence-based:

- **Instrument**: Recommend ITASH short form (21 items) for general digital technology attitudes; supplement with UTAUT constructs for adoption intention (Performance Expectancy, Effort Expectancy, Social Influence, Facilitating Conditions subscales — 4–5 items each); add a technostress module (3–5 items from the German Digital Stressors Scale) as a control variable. Total estimated instrument: 45–55 items on 5-point Likert scale.
- **Sample**: Clinical bedside nurses in Slovak hospitals and/or primary care settings; minimum N=300 for regression analysis with 8–10 predictors; stratified by age group (under/over 40), education level, and clinical setting.
- **Validation**: Factor analysis required for any translated instruments; CVI with expert panel recommended for content validity; test-retest reliability with 2-week interval on a subsample (N≥30).
- **Hypothesis testing**: H1 (technology use → efficiency perception) — multiple regression with PU as DV; H2 (education → adoption) — one-way ANOVA + regression with education level as IV; H3 (experience/age → attitudes) — correlation analysis (Spearman or Pearson) + moderation analysis.
