## Theoretical Background

### Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) and UTAUT2

**Originators:** Davis (1989); Venkatesh et al. (2003, 2012)

**Core content:** TAM posits that perceived usefulness and perceived ease of use predict technology adoption intention. UTAUT2 expands this with performance expectancy, effort expectancy, social influence, facilitating conditions, hedonic motivation, habit, and price value as determinants of behavioural intention and use behaviour.

**Relevance to this study:** Multiple reviewed studies (Shiyab et al., 2024; Alipour et al., 2025; Ahmadpour et al., 2025) use UTAUT/TAM constructs to explain why nurses adopt or resist mHealth apps in clinical settings. Understanding which constructs dominate informs implementation strategy.

### Expectation-Confirmation Model (ECM) of Information Systems Continuance

**Originator:** Bhattacherjee (2001)

**Core content:** Post-adoption continuance is determined by user satisfaction, which in turn depends on confirmation of prior expectations and perceived usefulness after actual use.

**Relevance to this study:** Ahmadpour et al. (2025) applied an extended ECM to Iranian nurses, providing a post-adoption lens that complements the initial-adoption focus of TAM/UTAUT. This is particularly relevant for understanding why nurses who initially adopt mHealth apps may or may not continue using them in clinical routine.

### Consolidated Framework for Implementation Research (CFIR)

**Originators:** Damschroder et al. (2009)

**Core content:** Multi-level framework covering intervention characteristics, outer setting, inner setting, characteristics of individuals, and implementation process as determinants of implementation success.

**Relevance to this study:** Ardito et al. (2023) applied CFIR to mHealth in cancer care, yielding a transferable taxonomy of barriers and facilitators directly applicable to nursing contexts in hospital systems.

## Research Gap Summary

1. **Slovak and Central European context absent:** No identified peer-reviewed study between 2021–2026 examined mHealth adoption, barriers, or outcomes specifically among Slovak nurses or in Slovak hospital settings. The majority of evidence originates from Australia, Iran, the United States, China, and Sub-Saharan Africa, with very limited European nursing-specific data. This absence constitutes the primary empirical justification for the proposed thesis.

2. **Clinical-to-personal use gap unaddressed at system level:** Studies document the gap between personal (68.4%) and clinical (under 30%) mHealth app use (Shiyab et al., 2024; Alipour et al., 2025) but do not provide institution-level intervention data demonstrating how organisational policies or structured training programmes could systematically close this gap in hospital environments.

3. **EU regulatory compliance of nursing apps unexamined:** While Mia et al. (2022) assessed HIPAA compliance and Alhammad et al. (2024) documented patient privacy concerns, no study reviewed the GDPR compliance of nurse-facing or patient-facing mHealth applications in clinical use within EU jurisdictions. Slovak implementation must operate under GDPR, yet no evidence base addresses this intersection.

4. **Long-term outcome data lacking:** Of 319 medication management app studies reviewed by Wang et al. (2025), the majority measured short-term adherence. Studies on nurse-led telehealth (Bulto, 2024) similarly lack 12+ month follow-up. The durability of mHealth-related improvements in nursing care quality remains empirically unsupported.

5. **Scalability and sustainability evidence absent:** Olomi et al. (2025) found that only 1 of 15 reviewed mHealth tools for maternal care achieved national scale. A comparable scalability deficit likely applies to nursing contexts broadly. The conditions under which mHealth solutions transition from pilot to standard clinical practice remain underspecified.

## Proposed Theoretical Framework

The study adopts an integrated framework combining UTAUT2 (Venkatesh et al., 2012) for initial adoption factors with Bhattacherjee's ECM for continuance intention, situated within the CFIR implementation context. This three-layer model positions mHealth adoption as a dynamic process:

- **Layer 1 – Initial Adoption (UTAUT2):** Performance expectancy, effort expectancy, social influence, and facilitating conditions determine whether nurses choose to use mHealth apps in clinical practice.
- **Layer 2 – Sustained Use (ECM):** Post-adoption confirmation of expectations, perceived usefulness, satisfaction, and habit determine whether initial adopters continue using apps as part of clinical routine.
- **Layer 3 – Implementation Context (CFIR):** Inner setting factors (hospital IT infrastructure, leadership support, EHR compatibility) and outer setting factors (national regulation, patient digital literacy, professional standards) moderate the relationship between individual adoption intention and actual clinical behaviour.

This framework accommodates the Slovak hospital context by explicitly including regulatory environment (GDPR) and legacy HIS compatibility as contextual moderators, enabling the proposed research to generate actionable recommendations for nursing managers and health informaticians in Slovak clinical settings.
