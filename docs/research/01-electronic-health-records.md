## Theoretical Background

### 1. Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) — Davis (1989)
**Originator:** Fred D. Davis (1989), expanded to TAM-2 (Venkatesh & Davis, 2000) and TAM-3 (Venkatesh & Bala, 2008)
**Core content:** TAM posits that two core perceptions — perceived ease of use and perceived usefulness — are the primary determinants of an individual's intention to use a technology and actual usage behavior. In TAM-3, subjective norms and social influence were added as antecedents in organizational contexts.
**Relevance to this study:** TAM is the dominant framework for examining EHR acceptance among nurses. Studies (Alrasheeday et al., 2023; Corbett et al., 2025) consistently invoke perceived usefulness, ease of use, and self-efficacy as predictors of nurses' EHR adoption. TAM provides the theoretical lens through which nurses' attitudes and satisfaction data are interpreted.

### 2. Diffusion of Innovations Theory — Rogers (1962/2003)
**Originator:** Everett M. Rogers (1962; 5th ed. 2003)
**Core content:** Technology adoption spreads through a social system via communication channels. Adopters are classified as innovators, early adopters, early majority, late majority, and laggards. Key attributes of an innovation affecting its adoption rate include relative advantage, compatibility, complexity, trialability, and observability.
**Relevance to this study:** Explains why EHR adoption rates vary across nursing units, institutions, and countries (including Slovakia's slower EMRAM progression). The "superuser" peer-champion model identified in recent empirical work (Corbett et al., 2025; Asif & Khan, 2024) maps directly onto Rogers' early adopter and opinion leader constructs.

### 3. Documentation Burden Framework — American Nursing Informatics Association (2023)
**Originator:** American Nursing Informatics Association (ANIA), formalized 2022–2023
**Core content:** The Six Domains of Documentation Burden identify reimbursement, regulatory, quality, usability, interoperability/standards, and self-imposed factors as structural causes of excessive EHR workload in nursing. This framework moves beyond individual user attitudes to address systemic design failures.
**Relevance to this study:** Provides a conceptual vocabulary for analyzing how Slovak nursing institutions might experience EHR-related documentation burden, particularly in contexts where regulatory requirements and dual (paper + electronic) documentation systems overlap.

### 4. Translational Mobilization Theory — Allen (2014)
**Originator:** Davina Allen (2014), applied to EHR research by Bignell & Petrovskaya (2024)
**Core content:** Nursing involves substantial "invisible organizing work" — knowledge management, resource coordination, and patient flow facilitation — that is often unacknowledged in formal documentation systems.
**Relevance to this study:** Explains why EHR interfaces designed for physician charting frequently fail to capture the full scope of nursing labor, which has implications for workflow studies in Slovak hospital settings.

## Research Gap Summary

**1. Absence of Slovak-specific empirical nursing EHR research:** No peer-reviewed study identified in any searched database focused specifically on nurses' experiences, attitudes, or competencies with the Slovak ezdravie system or hospital nursing information systems (Prosoft, CARDIS, etc.). The only Slovak-relevant primary study found (Pajari et al., 2025) examines nurse educators' digital competence in a four-country sample, not EHR use in clinical practice. This represents a critical empirical void.

**2. Dual documentation burden in transitional systems is understudied:** The Tunisian quasi-experimental study (Chouchene Douma et al., 2024) is one of the very few experimental designs documenting the decline in EHR usage during transition (67.5% → 51.4% at 6 months). Slovak hospitals undergoing simultaneous paper-and-electronic documentation requirements represent a comparable transitional context that has not been studied. No study quantifies the documentation overhead cost in Central European public hospital systems.

**3. Holistic and psychosocial dimensions of nursing documentation in EHR are underrepresented:** The Slovenian grounded theory study (Krel & Vrbnjak, 2025) is the only Central European source documenting the specific erosion of psychosocial and spiritual care documentation under electronic systems. No Slovak nursing study examines whether ezdravie's structured data entry supports or suppresses the nursing process's assessment of psychosocial needs.

**4. Nursing informatics education and EHR training curricula in Slovakia lack evaluation:** While Pajari et al. (2025) assesses digital competence of Slovak nurse educators, no study evaluates whether Slovak undergraduate or graduate nursing curricula adequately prepare students for clinical EHR use. Given that 53% of Finnish nurses reported insufficient EHR training (Heponiemi et al., 2021) in a digitally more mature system, this gap is likely larger in Slovakia.

**5. Measurement standardization gap:** Moy et al. (2021) established that validated measures of nursing documentation burden are lacking in the international literature. This methodological gap extends to the Slovak context, where no standardized Slovak-language instrument for measuring nurses' EHR documentation burden or satisfaction exists.

## Proposed Theoretical Framework

The study proposes an integrated framework combining three complementary theoretical lenses:

**Level 1 — Individual (TAM):** Perceived ease of use + perceived usefulness + self-efficacy → intention to use → actual EHR use behavior. This captures individual nurse attitudes and predicts adoption patterns across demographic subgroups (age, experience, education level).

**Level 2 — Organizational (ANIA Documentation Burden Framework):** Six structural domains (regulatory, reimbursement, quality reporting, usability, interoperability, self-imposed) mediate the gap between intended EHR benefits and actual nursing experience. This level explains why positive individual attitudes may not translate into effective use when system design and organizational pressures are misaligned.

**Level 3 — Systemic (Diffusion of Innovations + EHDS):** Macro-level adoption trajectories, peer champion dynamics, and EU regulatory requirements frame the evolution of EHR use over time and across Slovak healthcare institutions.

## Handoff Notes

**To methodology expert:** The dominant methodology in this literature is cross-sectional survey (for attitudes/barriers) and systematic/scoping review (for effectiveness evidence). Only one quasi-experimental study (Chouchene Douma et al., 2024) provides causal evidence. For the Slovak thesis, a cross-sectional descriptive-analytical survey using the NATC questionnaire (used by Alrasheeday et al., 2023) or a modified TAM instrument is recommended as the primary data collection instrument. Sample should be drawn from Slovak hospital nurses across at least 2–3 facility types (university hospital, district hospital, primary care). Given the absence of a validated Slovak-language EHR burden instrument, the study may include translation and adaptation of the BurDoNsaM (Gesner et al., 2022) as a secondary contribution.

**To writing coach:** The thesis can be framed around the tension between EHR's demonstrated patient safety benefits and its equally documented nursing workforce costs (burnout, documentation burden, erosion of holistic care).
