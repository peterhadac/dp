# Research Methodology Design

## Research Design Overview
- **Research approach**: Quantitative
- **Research type**: Non-experimental, descriptive-analytical survey
- **Rationale**: The research questions concern the prevalence, frequency, and correlates of digital tool use among a defined professional population. Quantitative methods allow for standardized measurement across a sufficiently large sample, enable statistical testing of hypothesized relationships, and yield findings that can be generalised — within defined limits — to the target population of Slovak nurses. A non-experimental design is appropriate because the independent variables (education level, years of experience) cannot be manipulated and because the aim is to describe and analyse an existing phenomenon rather than establish causal directionality under controlled conditions.

## Methods Chapter — Full Academic Text

### 3.1 Research Type and Approach

This study adopts a quantitative, non-experimental, descriptive-analytical design implemented through a cross-sectional survey. Quantitative methodology was selected because the primary objective is to measure the frequency and extent of digital tool use among nurses, to identify statistically significant relationships between demographic and professional variables and technology-related attitudes and behaviours, and to produce numerical data suitable for inferential testing. A cross-sectional design was considered optimal given practical constraints of time and resource availability at the master's thesis level, and because no manipulation of variables is required: the independent variables of interest — specifically level of completed nursing education and years of clinical experience — are pre-existing characteristics of respondents that are observed rather than assigned.

The survey instrument takes the form of a non-standardised, self-constructed questionnaire. This choice reflects the absence of a validated Slovak-language instrument precisely tailored to the construct of digital technology use in contemporary nursing practice. Although standardised tools exist for related constructs such as technology acceptance (e.g., the Technology Acceptance Model questionnaire) and health informatics self-efficacy, none adequately covers the full range of digital tools currently deployed in Slovak inpatient and outpatient settings, including electronic health record (EHR) systems, clinical decision-support applications, telemedicine platforms, and mobile health devices. The development of a purpose-built instrument also permits alignment of item content with the specific research goals and hypotheses formulated for this study.

### 3.2 Research Problem Statement

The rapid integration of digital technologies into healthcare systems worldwide has fundamentally altered the landscape of nursing practice. In Slovakia, progressive digitalisation of hospital and outpatient documentation, the introduction of national EHR infrastructure, and the increasing availability of mobile clinical applications have placed new cognitive and technical demands on the nursing workforce. Despite these developments, empirical data on the actual frequency of digital tool use among Slovak nurses, the relationship between nurses' educational background and their technology adoption behaviour, and nurses' own assessments of how these tools affect their work efficiency and patient care quality remain scarce. This evidence gap hampers evidence-based policy-making regarding nursing education curricula, workforce training programmes, and healthcare IT investment priorities. The present study addresses this gap by conducting a systematic empirical survey of nurses working across inpatient and outpatient settings in Slovakia.

### 3.3 Research Goals

**Main research goal:** To analyse the use of modern technologies and digital tools in nursing practice in Slovakia, with a focus on frequency of use, perceived impact on work efficiency and patient care quality, and the relationship between nurses' characteristics and their technology-related attitudes and behaviours.

**Partial research goals:**

1. To determine the frequency and types of digital tools used by nurses in their daily clinical practice across inpatient and outpatient settings.
2. To assess nurses' self-rated perception of the impact of digital tools on their personal work efficiency.
3. To examine whether a statistically significant relationship exists between the frequency of digital tool use and nurses' self-rated work efficiency.
4. To compare the frequency of modern technology use between nurses with higher education (bachelor's degree or higher) and nurses with secondary vocational education.
5. To determine what proportion of nurses perceive EHR systems as contributing to improved quality and safety of patient care, and to test whether this proportion exceeds fifty percent.

### 3.4 Research Hypotheses

Four research hypotheses were formulated on the basis of the theoretical framework established in the literature review and the identified research gaps. Each hypothesis is stated in its alternative form; the null hypothesis of no association or no difference is the form subjected to statistical testing.

**H1:** There is a statistically significant relationship between the frequency of digital tool use and nurses' self-rated work efficiency.

*Operational definitions:* Frequency of digital tool use is operationalised as the respondent's self-reported frequency of using any digital tool (EHR, clinical decision support, telemedicine, mobile health application, diagnostic device with digital interface) in clinical practice, measured on a five-point ordinal scale ranging from "never" to "several times per day." Self-rated work efficiency is operationalised as the respondent's overall subjective assessment of how digital tools affect their personal work efficiency, measured on a five-point Likert scale ranging from "markedly decreases efficiency" to "markedly increases efficiency." A statistically significant relationship is defined as a Chi-square test result yielding a p-value below 0.05.

**H2:** Nurses with higher education (bachelor's degree or above) use modern technologies more frequently than nurses with secondary vocational nursing education.

*Operational definitions:* Level of education is operationalised as a dichotomous variable derived from the respondent's self-reported highest completed nursing qualification: (a) secondary vocational education (Slovak: stredoškolské vzdelanie — maturita) and (b) higher education, encompassing bachelor's (Bc.), master's (Mgr., Mgr. art.), or doctoral (PhD.) degree programmes. Frequency of use is operationalised as described in H1. For the purposes of Chi-square analysis, the five-point frequency scale is collapsed into two categories: lower frequency (never / rarely / occasionally) and higher frequency (often / several times per day).

**H3:** There is a statistically significant relationship between years of clinical experience and attitude toward digital technologies in nursing practice.

*Operational definitions:* Years of clinical experience is operationalised as the respondent's self-reported total duration of active nursing practice, recorded in years and subsequently categorised into four bands: 0–5 years, 6–10 years, 11–20 years, and more than 20 years. Attitude toward digital technologies is operationalised as the respondent's overall attitudinal orientation toward the use of digital tools in nursing, measured on a five-point Likert scale ranging from "strongly negative" to "strongly positive," subsequently collapsed into three categories (negative, neutral, positive) for Chi-square analysis.

**H4:** More than fifty percent of nurses rate EHR systems as contributing to improved quality and safety of patient care.

*Operational definition:* This hypothesis concerns a single proportion rather than an association between two variables. The relevant item asks respondents to rate the contribution of EHR to quality and safety of patient care on a five-point scale: "markedly worsens," "somewhat worsens," "no change," "somewhat improves," and "markedly improves." Positive assessment is operationalised as selection of either "somewhat improves" or "markedly improves." The hypothesis will be evaluated by calculating the observed proportion of positive responses and testing it against the threshold of 50% using a one-sample proportion test (z-test for proportions), with a significance level of α = 0.05.

### 3.5 Research Sample

**Target population:** All registered nurses (Slovak: diplomovaná sestra, všeobecná sestra) actively employed in inpatient healthcare facilities (hospitals, inpatient rehabilitation centres) or outpatient clinics (ambulancie) in Slovakia at the time of data collection.

**Inclusion criteria:**
- Completed nursing education at secondary vocational level or higher
- Active clinical employment in an inpatient or outpatient nursing position at time of survey
- Minimum of one year of clinical nursing experience
- Voluntary participation with provision of informed consent

**Exclusion criteria:**
- Nurses on long-term sick leave, parental leave, or administrative leave at time of data collection
- Student nurses who have not yet completed their qualifying examinations
- Nursing managers whose current role involves no direct patient care

**Sample size:** The target sample size is 150–200 completed questionnaires. This range is supported by a priori power analysis considerations: for a Chi-square test of independence with two degrees of freedom, a medium effect size (w = 0.30, following Cohen's convention), a significance level of α = 0.05, and desired power of 1 − β = 0.80, the minimum required sample size is approximately 108 respondents (calculated using G*Power 3.1). A target of 150–200 respondents provides an appropriate buffer for anticipated non-response and incomplete questionnaires (estimated attrition rate: 15–20%), ensures adequate statistical power for all four hypotheses tested, and allows for meaningful subgroup comparisons by education level and years of experience.

**Sampling method:** Convenience sampling combined with purposive criterion sampling was employed. This non-probability sampling strategy was adopted for pragmatic reasons: access to a national probability sample of Slovak nurses would require institutional registry data unavailable to an individual researcher, and the time frame of a master's thesis does not permit multi-stage cluster sampling across all Slovak regions. Questionnaires were distributed through personal contacts in healthcare facilities and through the network of colleagues willing to assist with data collection. To mitigate the geographic concentration bias inherent in convenience sampling, efforts were made to include facilities from at least three Slovak regions and to achieve representation from both inpatient and outpatient settings. The limitations of non-probability sampling are acknowledged in section 3.10.

### 3.6 Research Instrument

**Instrument description:** The data collection instrument is a non-standardised, self-administered questionnaire constructed by the researcher specifically for the purposes of this study. The questionnaire is structured into four thematic sections:

- *Section A — Sociodemographic and professional characteristics:* six items covering age, sex, highest completed nursing qualification, current type of workplace (inpatient / outpatient), years of clinical experience, and primary clinical department or specialisation.
- *Section B — Use of digital tools:* twelve items examining which specific digital tools the respondent uses (checklist format), the overall frequency of digital tool use (five-point ordinal scale), specific frequency of EHR use, and perceived adequacy of available digital infrastructure.
- *Section C — Perceived impact on work efficiency:* eight items assessing respondents' subjective evaluation of how specific categories of digital tool affect their efficiency, workload, and documentation burden, measured on five-point Likert scales.
- *Section D — Attitudes toward digital technologies and EHR:* ten items measuring general attitudinal orientation toward digitalisation in nursing, perceived contribution of EHR to patient care quality and safety, willingness to adopt new technologies, and perceived need for further digital skills training.

The questionnaire contains a total of 36 items. Items in Sections C and D employ five-point Likert-type scales. No reverse-scored items were included in the initial instrument version, pending pilot testing feedback.

**Construction process:** Item construction followed a systematic four-stage process. In stage one, a conceptual map of the relevant constructs was developed on the basis of the literature review, identifying key domains: technology types, frequency of use, efficiency, quality/safety, attitudes, and training needs. In stage two, an initial item pool of 52 candidate items was generated, deliberately oversampling to allow for item reduction following expert review. In stage three, content validity review was conducted informally by three subject-matter experts — a nursing educator with experience in health informatics, a senior clinical nurse employed in a hospital with a fully digitalised EHR system, and a methodologist familiar with survey design — who assessed each item for relevance, clarity, and appropriate language register. Items flagged as ambiguous, redundant, or outside the scope of the defined constructs were revised or removed, yielding the 36-item instrument described above. In stage four, pilot testing was conducted as described below.

**Pilot testing:** Prior to main data collection, the questionnaire was pilot-tested with a convenience sample of 8 nurses (5 employed in inpatient settings, 3 in outpatient settings) who met the inclusion criteria but were not subsequently included in the main study sample. Pilot participants were asked to complete the questionnaire independently and then to provide written feedback on item clarity, time required for completion, and any items perceived as confusing, offensive, or inapplicable to their practice. Estimated completion time reported by pilot participants ranged from 11 to 17 minutes (mean approximately 14 minutes). On the basis of pilot feedback, three items were reworded for clarity, one response scale label was revised, and one item pertaining to a technology not in widespread use in outpatient settings was restructured as a conditional item with a skip instruction.

**Reliability and validity:**

*Content validity* was addressed through the systematic expert review described above and through grounding of item content in the published literature on digital tool use in nursing. A content validity index was not formally computed due to the small number of expert reviewers; this is acknowledged as a limitation.

*Construct validity* was not formally assessed through factor analysis in this study, as the sample size, while adequate for the planned hypothesis tests, does not meet the recommended respondent-to-item ratio (typically 10:1) for stable exploratory factor analysis. The internal consistency of attitudinal scale items in Sections C and D will be assessed post hoc using Cronbach's alpha coefficient, with a value of α ≥ 0.70 considered acceptable for research purposes (Nunnally, 1978).

*Temporal stability (test-retest reliability)* was not assessed due to logistical constraints. This limitation is noted in section 3.10.

### 3.7 Data Collection Procedure

**Distribution method:** Questionnaires were distributed in two parallel formats to maximise response rate and to reach nurses with varying levels of access to digital devices during working hours. Printed paper questionnaires were distributed in person at selected healthcare facilities, where a designated contact person (typically a head nurse or ward manager who agreed to assist with distribution) made questionnaires available to eligible nurses during pre- or post-shift briefings. Electronic questionnaires were made available via Google Forms (Google LLC), a secure online platform, accessed through a hyperlink and QR code printed on the paper invitation letter. Respondents were free to choose their preferred format; both formats contained identical items in identical order.

**Timeline:** Data collection was planned to take place over a period of six to eight weeks. A reminder communication was issued to contact persons at each participating facility after four weeks to encourage return of completed paper questionnaires and to prompt nurses who had not yet accessed the electronic form.

**Ethical considerations and informed consent:** Prior to completing the questionnaire, each respondent was provided with a written information sheet (printed on the first page of the paper questionnaire and displayed as the first screen of the electronic form) explaining: the purpose and scope of the study, the institutional affiliation of the researcher, the voluntary nature of participation, the right to withdraw without consequence at any point prior to submission, the anonymous character of the questionnaire, the intended use of collected data solely for academic purposes, and the manner in which data would be stored and destroyed after the completion of the thesis. Completion and submission of the questionnaire constituted the respondent's expression of informed consent. No personal identifiers (name, employee number, precise workplace address) were collected at any point. The study did not involve the collection of patient data, clinical interventions, or biological specimens; accordingly, formal IRB or ethics committee review was not mandatory under applicable Slovak legal regulations for this category of research. Nevertheless, the research was conducted in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki principles as they apply to survey research involving human participants, and with the internal guidelines of the Faculty of Health Sciences and Social Work, Trnava University.

**Anonymity and data protection:** Paper questionnaires were collected in sealed envelopes and stored in a locked cabinet accessible only to the researcher. Electronic responses were stored within the researcher's password-protected Google account; raw response data were exported to a password-protected spreadsheet for analysis and will be deleted upon successful defence of the thesis. No data permitting identification of individual respondents or their employing institutions are reported in any part of this work.

### 3.8 Statistical Analysis Methods

All statistical analyses were performed using Microsoft Excel (Microsoft Corporation, version 365). Data from paper questionnaires were entered manually into the spreadsheet following verification of completeness; electronic responses were exported directly. Incomplete questionnaires (defined as questionnaires with more than 20% of substantive items unanswered) were excluded from analysis prior to any calculations.

**Descriptive statistics** were calculated for all variables. For categorical and ordinal variables, absolute frequencies (n) and relative frequencies (%) were calculated and presented in frequency tables and bar charts. For continuous variables (age, years of experience in their raw form prior to categorisation), the arithmetic mean, median, and range were reported. Measures of central tendency and dispersion were computed for Likert-scale composite scores where applicable.

**Inferential statistics:** The primary inferential technique applied in this study is the Pearson Chi-square test of independence (χ²), used to evaluate H1, H2, and H3. The Chi-square test examines whether an observed distribution of frequencies across the cells of a contingency table deviates significantly from the distribution that would be expected if the two variables were statistically independent. The test statistic is calculated as:

$$\chi^2 = \sum_{i=1}^{r} \sum_{j=1}^{c} \frac{(O_{ij} - E_{ij})^2}{E_{ij}}$$

where $O_{ij}$ denotes the observed frequency in cell $(i, j)$ of the contingency table, $E_{ij}$ denotes the expected frequency in that cell under the null hypothesis of independence (calculated as $E_{ij} = \frac{R_i \times C_j}{N}$, where $R_i$ is the row marginal total, $C_j$ is the column marginal total, and $N$ is the total sample size), $r$ is the number of rows, and $c$ is the number of columns.

The resulting test statistic follows a Chi-square distribution with degrees of freedom equal to $(r-1)(c-1)$. A p-value below the pre-specified significance threshold of α = 0.05 (two-tailed) was considered sufficient evidence to reject the null hypothesis of independence.

**Assumptions of the Chi-square test** were verified prior to interpretation of results: (1) observations are independent; (2) each cell of the contingency table has an expected frequency of at least 5. Where expected cell frequencies fell below 5 — which may occur in subgroup analyses with small counts — adjacent response categories were collapsed prior to testing, and the rationale for collapsing is reported in the Results section.

For **H4**, the proportion of nurses providing a positive EHR assessment was compared against the 50% threshold using a one-sample z-test for proportions:

$$z = \frac{\hat{p} - p_0}{\sqrt{\frac{p_0(1-p_0)}{n}}}$$

where $\hat{p}$ is the observed proportion, $p_0 = 0.50$ is the hypothesised proportion, and $n$ is the sample size. The result was evaluated against the standard normal distribution at α = 0.05 (one-tailed, as the hypothesis is directional).

Effect sizes were reported alongside significance test results: Cramér's V for Chi-square tests of independence (V = 0.10 small, 0.30 medium, 0.50 large, following Cohen, 1988) to contextualise the practical magnitude of statistically significant associations.

### 3.9 Validity and Reliability Considerations

The internal consistency of attitudinal scale items was assessed post hoc using Cronbach's alpha (α ≥ 0.70 threshold). Content validity was addressed through expert review and literature grounding during instrument construction. Face validity was supported by pilot testing with nurses from the target population. Potential threats to validity include social desirability bias (respondents may over-report positive attitudes toward digital tools if they perceive this to be the professionally expected response) and common method variance arising from the use of a single self-report instrument to measure both predictor and outcome variables. These threats are mitigated, but not eliminated, by the anonymous character of the questionnaire and by the wording of items to avoid leading language.

### 3.10 Limitations of the Study

1. **Convenience sampling** — The use of non-probability convenience sampling means that the sample may not be fully representative of the Slovak nursing workforce as a whole. Nurses at facilities with greater digital infrastructure or with a more proactive head nurse may be systematically overrepresented. Mitigation: facilities from multiple regions and both inpatient and outpatient settings were included; findings are interpreted with explicit acknowledgment of this constraint and are not generalised beyond the study sample without qualification.

2. **Self-report measurement** — All data, including frequency of technology use and self-rated efficiency, derive from respondents' subjective self-reports rather than from objective usage logs or performance metrics. Social desirability and recall biases may distort responses. Mitigation: the questionnaire was designed to be anonymous, and instructions emphasised that there were no correct or incorrect answers.

3. **Cross-sectional design** — A single time-point survey cannot establish the direction or temporal sequence of associations. Observed correlations between education or experience and technology use cannot be interpreted as causal. Mitigation: the study aims are explicitly descriptive and correlational; causal language is avoided throughout.

4. **Absence of test-retest reliability assessment** — The temporal stability of the instrument was not evaluated. Mitigation: internal consistency was assessed post hoc; future studies should include a test-retest component with a two to four week interval.

5. **Non-standardised instrument** — The self-constructed questionnaire lacks the established psychometric history of validated scales. Mitigation: systematic construction, expert review, and pilot testing were employed; Cronbach's alpha is reported; future research should pursue formal validation.

6. **Restriction to Slovak context** — Findings reflect the specific digital infrastructure, educational system, and healthcare organisation of Slovakia and may not transfer directly to other national settings.

## Research Ethics
- **IRB review**: Not formally required under Slovak regulations for anonymous survey research not involving patient data, clinical interventions, or biological material; study conducted in accordance with Declaration of Helsinki principles
