### 5.7 Limitations

This study was conducted with methodological rigour appropriate to the master's thesis level and to a first systematic empirical investigation in an underexplored national context. Nevertheless, several limitations must be acknowledged when interpreting the findings.

**Sampling method.** The use of non-probability convenience sampling, with questionnaire distribution conducted through personal contacts at a limited number of healthcare facilities, means that the study sample cannot be considered representative of the Slovak nursing workforce as a whole. Healthcare facilities with higher digital infrastructure maturity, or with head nurses who were personally motivated to support research participation, may be systematically overrepresented. Findings should therefore be interpreted as descriptive of the participating sample rather than as national prevalence estimates. Future research should employ stratified probability sampling based on facility type, geographic region, and hospital EMRAM stage.

**Self-report measurement.** All data, including the frequency of digital tool use and the self-rated efficiency assessment that together constitute the H1 variables, were obtained through self-report. Actual usage logs from facility information systems, which would provide objective frequency data independent of subjective recall bias, were not accessible within the parameters of this study. Social desirability bias — the tendency to report attitudes perceived as professionally appropriate — may have inflated positive efficiency ratings and attitudes toward digital tools, since digital literacy is increasingly valorised within the nursing profession. The anonymous questionnaire format mitigates but does not eliminate this risk.

**Cross-sectional design.** The cross-sectional nature of the survey captures a single temporal snapshot and cannot establish the direction or temporal ordering of observed associations. H1 tests the co-variation of usage frequency and efficiency rating, but whether higher frequency causes more positive efficiency appraisal (through habituation), or more positive efficiency appraisal motivates higher frequency (through voluntary adoption), or both are products of a common third variable such as institutional training quality, cannot be determined from the present data.

**Non-standardised instrument.** The self-constructed questionnaire has not been subjected to the full battery of psychometric validation procedures — including confirmatory factor analysis, criterion validity assessment against an established gold-standard measure, and test-retest reliability evaluation — that would be required for a fully validated instrument. Cronbach's alpha values [DATA PENDING: confirm whether thresholds were met] provide an initial indicator of internal consistency, but the absence of formal construct validation is a recognised limitation of novel questionnaire instruments at the master's thesis level.

### 5.8 Recommendations for Practice

The findings of this study, interpreted within the context of the theoretical framework and the international evidence base, support the following four concrete recommendations for nursing practice, nursing education, and healthcare administration in Slovakia.

**Recommendation 1: Implement structured, role-specific digital competency programmes as a mandatory component of continuing professional development for nurses.**

The international literature consistently identifies training adequacy as the primary modifiable determinant of nurses' positive technology attitudes and efficient digital tool use (Walzer et al., 2025; Jacques et al., 2025; Campione & Liu, 2024). In the Slovak context, where the eHealth Act mandates EHR use but does not specify minimum informatics competency standards for nursing staff, a competency gap between regulatory expectation and workforce capability persists. Healthcare facilities should adopt a competency-based digital skills framework — such as the TIGER framework as adapted for the European context, or the DigComp 2.2 proficiency structure — and provide differentiated training pathways based on nurses' baseline digital literacy, qualification level, and clinical role. Continuing education credits for digital skills training would incentivise engagement and create a professional development record analogous to clinical competency maintenance requirements.

**Recommendation 2: Develop targeted digital retraining programmes for nurses with ten or more years of clinical experience, using peer-mentoring models.**

[DATA PENDING: Adjust framing depending on whether H3 was confirmed or not.] The literature identifies experienced nurses — particularly those trained before digital documentation became standard in their clinical environment — as the subgroup most likely to hold neutral or negative attitudes toward digital technologies, and least likely to self-initiate digital skills development (De Leeuw, Woltjer & Kool, 2020; Mabaso, Mokonyama & Mitonga-Monga, 2025). Reverse mentoring programmes, in which digitally proficient junior nurses are formally paired with senior colleagues to share informatics skills while receiving clinical wisdom in return, have demonstrated effectiveness in reducing this generational competency gap with minimal institutional cost (Madhavanprabhakaran, Francis & Labrague, 2022; Robertson & Ferguson, 2025). Healthcare facilities should formalise these arrangements within ward rotation structures, providing protected time for mentoring sessions and recognising both mentors and mentees in professional development records.

**Recommendation 3: Prioritise EHR usability improvements and dual-documentation burden reduction in healthcare facility IT governance.**

The proportion of nurses who assessed EHR as [DATA PENDING: not beneficial / ambivalently beneficial] in this study reflects, at least in part, the usability and integration limitations of current EHR deployments rather than objectively poor safety outcomes. International evidence demonstrates that EHR redesign focused on reducing redundant documentation tasks, minimising alert fatigue, and eliminating paper shadow-record workarounds directly increases nursing satisfaction with and perceived benefit from digital systems (Jacques et al., 2025; Volkan et al., 2024). The eZdravie infrastructure should be extended to eliminate dual documentation requirements currently mandating both paper and electronic records in facilities at intermediate EMRAM stages. Healthcare facility IT committees should include frontline nurse representatives in procurement, configuration, and upgrade decisions, to ensure that interface design reflects actual nursing workflow needs rather than administrative or physician-centric documentation frameworks.

**Recommendation 4: Embed nursing informatics competencies across all levels of nursing education, with explicit alignment to the TIGER and DigComp frameworks.**

The observed association between educational qualification level and frequency of digital tool use — [DATA PENDING: confirmed / observed directionally even if non-significant] — indicates that formal education is a meaningful channel through which digital competency is acquired and sustained. Nursing curricula at secondary vocational level should include structured modules on EHR navigation, electronic documentation standards, and basic health informatics. Bachelor's-level programmes should develop information literacy and clinical data interpretation competencies. Master's-level programmes — including the programme from which this thesis emerges — should prepare graduates for digital leadership roles, including competency assessment, staff training design, and participation in healthcare IT governance. Alignment of Slovak nursing curricula with the TIGER competency framework and the EU DigComp 2.2 proficiency levels would position the Slovak nursing workforce for the digital interoperability requirements mandated by Regulation (EU) 2025/327 by March 2029.
