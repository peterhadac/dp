## Chapter 3: Research Methods

### 3.1 Research Type and Approach

This study adopts a quantitative, non-experimental, descriptive-analytical design implemented through a cross-sectional survey. Quantitative methodology was selected because the primary objective is to measure the frequency and extent of digital tool use among nurses, to identify statistically significant relationships between demographic and professional variables and technology-related attitudes and behaviours, and to produce numerical data amenable to inferential testing. Quantitative survey designs are well established in health services research as the appropriate paradigm when the aim is to describe the distribution of a phenomenon across a defined population and to test directional hypotheses about associations among measured variables (Polit & Beck, 2021).

A cross-sectional design was considered optimal given the practical constraints of time and resource availability at the master's thesis level, and because no manipulation of variables is required or ethically appropriate in this context. The independent variables of primary interest — specifically the level of completed nursing education and the number of years of active clinical experience — are pre-existing, non-modifiable characteristics of respondents that are observed and recorded rather than experimentally assigned. A cross-sectional survey is the standard design for this type of prevalence and association research (Kelley et al., 2003).

The survey instrument takes the form of a non-standardised, self-constructed questionnaire developed specifically for this study. This decision reflects the absence of a validated Slovak-language instrument sufficiently tailored to the construct of digital technology use in contemporary nursing practice. While standardised scales exist for related constructs — most notably the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) questionnaire (Davis, 1989) and various health informatics self-efficacy instruments — none of these tools adequately encompasses the full range of digital tools currently deployed in Slovak inpatient and outpatient nursing environments, including electronic health record (EHR) systems, clinical decision-support applications, telemedicine platforms, and mobile health devices. The construction of a purpose-built instrument also enabled precise alignment of item content with the specific research goals and hypotheses formulated for this investigation.

### 3.2 Research Problem Statement

The progressive integration of digital technologies into healthcare systems worldwide has fundamentally altered the scope and character of nursing practice. In Slovakia, the digitalisation of hospital and outpatient documentation, the introduction of national electronic health record infrastructure, and the increasing availability of mobile clinical applications have collectively imposed new cognitive and technical demands on the nursing workforce. Nurses are now required not only to acquire and maintain digital competencies alongside their clinical skills, but also to adapt to workflow changes driven by institutional technology adoption that may or may not be accompanied by adequate training.

Despite these developments, systematic empirical data on the actual frequency of digital tool use among Slovak nurses, the relationship between nurses' educational background and their technology adoption behaviour, and nurses' own evaluations of the impact of these tools on work efficiency and patient care quality remain scarce in the published literature. The evidence available in international sources is not straightforwardly transferable to the Slovak context, given the country's specific pattern of healthcare IT infrastructure rollout, its particular nursing education system spanning secondary vocational and university pathways, and its distinct healthcare organisational structure. This evidence gap constrains evidence-based decision-making regarding nursing education curricula, workforce digital literacy programmes, and healthcare information technology investment priorities. The present study is designed to address this gap by conducting a systematic empirical survey of nurses working across inpatient and outpatient settings in Slovakia.

### 3.3 Research Goals

The main research goal of this study is to analyse the use of modern technologies and digital tools in nursing practice in Slovakia, with a focus on the frequency and type of use, nurses' perceived impact of these tools on work efficiency and patient care quality, and the relationship between nurses' professional and educational characteristics and their technology-related attitudes and behaviours.

Five partial research goals guide the operationalisation of this main goal. The first partial goal is to determine the frequency and types of digital tools used by nurses in their daily clinical practice across inpatient and outpatient settings. The second is to assess nurses' self-rated perception of the impact of digital tools on their personal work efficiency. The third is to examine whether a statistically significant relationship exists between the frequency of digital tool use and nurses' self-rated work efficiency. The fourth is to compare the frequency of modern technology use between nurses with higher education (bachelor's degree or above) and nurses with secondary vocational nursing education. The fifth is to determine what proportion of nurses perceive EHR systems as contributing to improved quality and safety of patient care, and to test whether this proportion exceeds fifty percent.

### 3.4 Research Hypotheses

Four research hypotheses were formulated on the basis of the theoretical framework established in the literature review and the identified empirical gaps in Slovak nursing informatics research. Each hypothesis is stated in its alternative (directional or existential) form; the corresponding null hypothesis of no association or no difference constitutes the form subjected to statistical testing, and is rejected if the calculated p-value falls below the pre-specified significance level of α = 0.05.

**Hypothesis 1 (H1):** There is a statistically significant relationship between the frequency of digital tool use and nurses' self-rated work efficiency.

For the purposes of this hypothesis, frequency of digital tool use is operationalised as the respondent's self-reported frequency of using any digital tool — including EHR systems, clinical decision-support applications, telemedicine platforms, mobile health applications, and diagnostic devices with digital interfaces — in clinical practice, measured on a five-point ordinal scale ranging from "never" to "several times per day." Self-rated work efficiency is operationalised as the respondent's overall subjective assessment of how digital tools affect their personal work efficiency, measured on a five-point Likert scale ranging from "markedly decreases efficiency" to "markedly increases efficiency." A statistically significant relationship is defined as a Chi-square test result yielding a p-value below 0.05.

**Hypothesis 2 (H2):** Nurses with higher education (bachelor's degree or above) use modern technologies more frequently than nurses with secondary vocational nursing education.

Level of education is operationalised as a dichotomous variable derived from the respondent's self-reported highest completed nursing qualification: (a) secondary vocational education (Slovak: *stredoškolské vzdelanie* — maturita) and (b) higher education, encompassing bachelor's (Bc.), master's (Mgr.), or doctoral (PhD.) degree programmes. Frequency of digital tool use is operationalised as described under H1. For the purposes of Chi-square analysis, the five-point frequency scale is collapsed into two ordinal categories: lower frequency (responses of "never," "rarely," or "occasionally") and higher frequency (responses of "often" or "several times per day").

**Hypothesis 3 (H3):** There is a statistically significant relationship between years of clinical experience and attitude toward digital technologies in nursing practice.

Years of clinical experience is operationalised as the respondent's self-reported total duration of active nursing practice, recorded in years and subsequently categorised into four bands: 0–5 years, 6–10 years, 11–20 years, and more than 20 years. Attitude toward digital technologies is operationalised as the respondent's overall attitudinal orientation toward the use of digital tools in nursing, measured on a five-point Likert scale ranging from "strongly negative" to "strongly positive," subsequently collapsed into three categories (negative, neutral, positive) for Chi-square analysis.

**Hypothesis 4 (H4):** More than fifty percent of nurses rate EHR systems as contributing to improved quality and safety of patient care.

This hypothesis concerns a single population proportion rather than an association between two variables, and accordingly requires a different analytical approach from H1 through H3. The relevant questionnaire item asks respondents to rate the contribution of EHR systems to the quality and safety of patient care on a five-point scale: "markedly worsens," "somewhat worsens," "no change," "somewhat improves," and "markedly improves." A positive assessment is operationalised as the selection of either "somewhat improves" or "markedly improves." The hypothesis is evaluated by calculating the observed proportion of respondents providing a positive rating and testing it against the null-hypothesis threshold of 50% using a one-sample z-test for proportions at the significance level of α = 0.05, one-tailed.

### 3.5 Research Sample

**Target Population.** The target population for this study consists of all registered nurses (Slovak: *diplomovaná sestra*, *všeobecná sestra*) actively employed in inpatient healthcare facilities — hospitals and inpatient rehabilitation centres — or outpatient clinics (*ambulancie*) in Slovakia at the time of data collection.

**Inclusion Criteria.** To be eligible for participation, respondents were required to satisfy all of the following criteria: completion of nursing education at secondary vocational level or higher; active clinical employment in an inpatient or outpatient nursing position at the time of survey completion; a minimum of one year of active clinical nursing experience; and voluntary participation with provision of informed consent.

**Exclusion Criteria.** Nurses on long-term sick leave, parental leave, or administrative leave at the time of data collection were excluded, as their current contact with digital clinical tools could not be assumed to reflect active practice. Student nurses who had not yet completed qualifying examinations were excluded on the grounds that their practice context differs materially from that of licensed clinical staff. Nursing managers whose current role involves no direct patient care were likewise excluded, as the study focuses specifically on the technology use experiences of nurses engaged in bedside or ambulatory clinical practice.

**Sample Size Determination.** The target sample size was set at 150 to 200 completed questionnaires. This range is supported by a priori power analysis: for a Pearson Chi-square test of independence with two degrees of freedom, a medium effect size (Cohen's w = 0.30), a two-tailed significance level of α = 0.05, and desired statistical power of 1 − β = 0.80, the minimum required sample size is approximately 108 respondents, as computed using G*Power 3.1 (Faul et al., 2007). A target of 150 to 200 respondents provides an appropriate attrition buffer for anticipated non-response and incomplete questionnaires, estimated at 15 to 20 percent, while ensuring adequate statistical power across all four hypotheses and allowing for meaningful subgroup comparisons by education level and years of experience.

**Sampling Method.** Given the absence of a centrally accessible registry of all actively employed Slovak nurses that would permit probability-based sampling, a combination of convenience sampling and purposive criterion sampling was employed. This non-probability strategy was adopted for pragmatic reasons consistent with master's thesis research: constructing a true probability sample from the national nursing workforce would require institutional data unavailable to an individual researcher, and the time frame of the degree programme does not permit multi-stage cluster sampling across Slovak regions. Questionnaires were distributed through personal professional contacts in healthcare facilities and through colleagues willing to facilitate distribution within their institutions. To mitigate the geographic concentration bias inherent in convenience sampling, deliberate efforts were made to include facilities from at least three Slovak regions and to achieve balanced representation from both inpatient and outpatient settings. The limitations of non-probability sampling and their implications for generalisation are addressed explicitly in section 3.10.

### 3.6 Research Instrument

**Instrument Overview.** The data collection instrument is a self-administered questionnaire constructed by the researcher specifically for the purposes of this study. The questionnaire comprises 36 items organised into four thematic sections, designed to be completed in approximately 12 to 15 minutes.

**Section A — Sociodemographic and Professional Characteristics** consists of six items covering age, sex, highest completed nursing qualification, current type of workplace (inpatient or outpatient), years of active clinical experience, and primary clinical department or area of specialisation. Responses to Section A items provide the independent and control variables for hypothesis testing and subgroup analyses.

**Section B — Use of Digital Tools** comprises twelve items examining which specific digital tools the respondent uses in clinical practice (checklist format permitting multiple selections), the overall frequency of digital tool use (five-point ordinal scale), the specific frequency of EHR system use, and the respondent's perceived adequacy of the digital infrastructure available at their workplace.

**Section C — Perceived Impact on Work Efficiency** includes eight items assessing respondents' subjective evaluation of how specific categories of digital tool affect their personal work efficiency, documentation burden, and clinical workload, measured on five-point Likert scales anchored at "markedly decreases" and "markedly increases." These items provide the dependent variable for H1.

**Section D — Attitudes Toward Digital Technologies and EHR** contains ten items measuring respondents' general attitudinal orientation toward digitalisation in nursing practice, their perceived contribution of EHR systems to patient care quality and safety, their willingness to adopt new digital tools, and their perceived need for further digital skills training. The EHR quality and safety rating item in this section provides the dependent variable for H4, and the overall attitudinal orientation scale provides the dependent variable for H3.

**Instrument Construction Process.** The development of the questionnaire followed a systematic four-stage process. In the first stage, a conceptual map of the relevant constructs was developed on the basis of the literature review, identifying six key domains: types of digital tools used in nursing practice, frequency of use, perceived impact on work efficiency, perceived impact on patient care quality and safety, attitudinal orientation toward digitalisation, and training needs. In the second stage, an initial item pool of 52 candidate items was generated across these domains, deliberately oversampling to allow for subsequent reduction following expert review. In the third stage, content validity was addressed through informal expert review conducted by three subject-matter consultants: a nursing educator with expertise in health informatics, a senior clinical nurse employed at a facility with a fully digitalised EHR system, and a researcher familiar with survey instrument design. Each expert assessed all items for thematic relevance, clarity of wording, and appropriateness of response scale format. Items identified as ambiguous, redundant, or falling outside the defined construct boundaries were revised or removed, yielding the 36-item instrument described above. In the fourth stage, the instrument underwent pilot testing with a small sample of target-population members prior to main data collection.

**Pilot Testing.** Prior to commencing main data collection, the questionnaire was administered to a convenience pilot sample of eight nurses — five employed in inpatient settings and three in outpatient settings — who met all inclusion criteria but were not subsequently included in the main study sample. Pilot participants completed the questionnaire independently under conditions comparable to the main study and then provided written feedback on item clarity, estimated completion time, and any items perceived as confusing, ambiguous, or inapplicable to their clinical practice. Estimated completion time ranged from 11 to 17 minutes across pilot participants, with a mean of approximately 14 minutes. On the basis of the feedback received, three items were reworded to improve clarity, one response scale label was revised to better distinguish adjacent categories, and one item concerning a technology not in widespread use in outpatient settings was restructured as a conditional item accompanied by an explicit skip instruction, to avoid inapplicable burden for respondents in ambulatory roles.

**Reliability and Validity.** Content validity was addressed through the systematic expert review and the grounding of item content in the published empirical literature on digital tool use in nursing, as detailed above. A formal content validity index (CVI) was not computed owing to the small number of expert reviewers; this is acknowledged as a limitation. Construct validity was not formally assessed through confirmatory or exploratory factor analysis, as the study sample size, while adequate for the planned hypothesis tests, does not meet the respondent-to-item ratio of at least 10:1 recommended for stable exploratory factor solutions (Hair et al., 2019). The internal consistency of attitudinal scale items in Sections C and D was assessed post hoc using Cronbach's alpha coefficient; a coefficient of α ≥ 0.70 was adopted as the threshold for acceptable internal consistency for research purposes (Nunnally, 1978). Temporal stability was not assessed through test-retest administration owing to logistical constraints; this limitation is acknowledged in section 3.10.

### 3.7 Data Collection Procedure

**Distribution Method.** Questionnaires were distributed in two parallel formats — printed paper and electronic — to maximise response rate and to accommodate nurses with differing levels of access to personal digital devices during working hours. Printed paper questionnaires were distributed in person at participating healthcare facilities, where a designated contact person — typically a head nurse or ward manager who had agreed in advance to assist with distribution — made questionnaires available to eligible nurses during pre-shift or post-shift briefings. Electronic questionnaires were made available via Google Forms (Google LLC), accessed through a hyperlink and QR code printed on the paper cover letter accompanying the physical questionnaires. Respondents were free to select their preferred format; both versions contained identical items presented in identical sequence.

**Timeline.** Main data collection was conducted over a period of six to eight weeks. A follow-up reminder communication was issued to contact persons at each participating facility approximately four weeks after initial distribution, with the aim of encouraging completion of outstanding paper questionnaires and prompting nurses who had not yet accessed the electronic form.

**Ethical Considerations and Informed Consent.** Each respondent received a written information sheet — printed on the cover page of the paper questionnaire and displayed as the introductory screen of the electronic form — explaining the purpose and scope of the study, the institutional affiliation of the researcher, the voluntary nature of participation and the right to withdraw at any point prior to questionnaire submission without any professional consequence, the anonymous character of all data collected, the exclusive use of the collected data for academic research purposes, and the procedures by which data would be stored and disposed of following completion of the thesis. Completion and submission of the questionnaire constituted the respondent's expression of freely given informed consent. No personal identifiers of any kind — including name, employee number, or precise workplace address — were collected at any point in the study.

The study did not involve the collection of patient data, the administration of clinical interventions, or the collection of biological specimens. Accordingly, formal institutional review board (IRB) or ethics committee approval was not a legal requirement under applicable Slovak regulations for this category of anonymous survey research. Nevertheless, the study was conducted in full accordance with the ethical principles of the Declaration of Helsinki as they apply to survey research involving adult human participants, and with the research ethics guidelines of the Faculty of Health Sciences and Social Work, Trnava University.

**Anonymity and Data Protection.** All procedures were designed to ensure the anonymity of participants and the security of data, consistent with the requirements of Regulation (EU) 2016/679 (General Data Protection Regulation, GDPR). Paper questionnaires were collected in sealed envelopes and stored in a locked cabinet accessible only to the researcher throughout the data collection and analysis period. Electronic responses were retained within the researcher's password-protected institutional Google account; raw response data were exported to a password-protected spreadsheet for statistical analysis. No data enabling the identification of individual respondents or their employing institutions are reported or reproduced in any part of this thesis. All raw data will be securely deleted upon successful defence of the thesis.

### 3.8 Statistical Analysis Methods

All statistical analyses were performed using Microsoft Excel (Microsoft Corporation, version 365). Data recorded on paper questionnaires were entered manually into the analysis spreadsheet following systematic verification of completeness; data submitted via the electronic form were exported directly. Questionnaires with more than 20 percent of substantive items unanswered were classified as incomplete and excluded from analysis prior to any calculations.

**Descriptive Statistics.** Descriptive statistics were calculated for all variables. For categorical and ordinal variables, absolute frequencies (*n*) and relative frequencies (%) were computed and presented in frequency tables complemented by bar charts. For continuous variables — specifically age and years of clinical experience in their raw (pre-categorised) form — the arithmetic mean, median, and range were reported. Measures of central tendency and dispersion were also computed for Likert-scale composite scores where appropriate.

**Inferential Statistics.** The primary inferential technique employed in this study is the Pearson Chi-square test of independence (χ²), applied to test H1, H2, and H3. The Chi-square test evaluates whether the observed distribution of frequencies across the cells of a contingency table departs significantly from the distribution that would be expected if the two variables under examination were statistically independent. The test statistic is calculated as:

$$\chi^2 = \sum_{i=1}^{r} \sum_{j=1}^{c} \frac{(O_{ij} - E_{ij})^2}{E_{ij}}$$

where $O_{ij}$ denotes the observed frequency in cell $(i, j)$ of the contingency table; $E_{ij}$ denotes the expected frequency in that cell under the null hypothesis of independence, calculated as $E_{ij} = \frac{R_i \times C_j}{N}$, where $R_i$ is the row marginal total, $C_j$ is the column marginal total, and $N$ is the total sample size; $r$ is the number of rows; and $c$ is the number of columns. The resulting test statistic follows a Chi-square distribution with degrees of freedom equal to $(r-1)(c-1)$. A p-value below the pre-specified significance level of α = 0.05 (two-tailed) was considered sufficient evidence to reject the null hypothesis of statistical independence.

Prior to interpreting each Chi-square result, the standard assumptions of the test were verified: (1) all observations are independent of one another; (2) each cell of the contingency table carries an expected frequency of at least five. Where expected cell frequencies fell below five — which may occur in subgroup analyses involving small counts within particular categories — adjacent response categories were collapsed prior to testing, and the rationale for any such collapsing is reported in the Results chapter.

For **Hypothesis 4**, which concerns a single population proportion rather than an association between two variables, the proportion of nurses providing a positive EHR quality and safety assessment was compared against the 50 percent threshold using a one-sample z-test for proportions:

$$z = \frac{\hat{p} - p_0}{\sqrt{\frac{p_0(1-p_0)}{n}}}$$

where $\hat{p}$ is the observed sample proportion of positive responses, $p_0 = 0.50$ is the proportion specified under the null hypothesis, and $n$ is the total sample size. The resulting test statistic was evaluated against the standard normal distribution at α = 0.05, one-tailed, in accordance with the directional character of the hypothesis.

**Effect Size Reporting.** Statistical significance alone does not convey the practical or clinical relevance of an observed association; therefore, effect sizes were calculated and reported alongside all significance test results. For Chi-square tests of independence, Cramér's V was computed as the effect size index, with the following interpretive benchmarks following Cohen (1988): V = 0.10 indicating a small effect, V = 0.30 a medium effect, and V = 0.50 a large effect. Reporting both p-values and effect sizes enables the reader to distinguish statistically significant but practically negligible associations from those of substantive magnitude.

### 3.9 Validity and Reliability Considerations

The validity and reliability of the study's findings depend in part on the psychometric properties of the research instrument and in part on the rigour of the data collection and analysis procedures. Content validity was addressed during the instrument construction phase through a systematic review of the item pool by three subject-matter experts and through close grounding of item content in the existing empirical literature. Face validity was supported by pilot testing with nurses drawn from the target population, who confirmed that items were intelligible and relevant to clinical nursing practice.

The internal consistency of attitudinal scale items in Sections C and D of the questionnaire was assessed post hoc using Cronbach's alpha (α ≥ 0.70 accepted as adequate for research-level measurement; Nunnally, 1978). Construct validity was not formally evaluated through factor analysis, as the study sample is not large enough relative to the number of scale items to support a reliable factor solution.

Two principal threats to internal validity were identified. The first is social desirability bias: respondents may systematically over-report positive attitudes toward digital tools and higher frequencies of use if they perceive such responses to be the professionally expected or desirable answers. This threat was mitigated through the fully anonymous design of the questionnaire and through the explicit instruction, provided on the information sheet, that all responses are equally valid and that there are no correct or incorrect answers. The second threat is common method variance, arising from the use of a single self-report instrument to measure both predictor and outcome variables within the same administration session. This cannot be fully eliminated in a single-wave survey design; however, the anonymous character of the questionnaire and the neutral, non-leading wording of all items reduce its likely magnitude. These limitations are discussed further in the following section.

### 3.10 Limitations of the Study

Six principal methodological limitations of this study are acknowledged, each accompanied by the mitigation strategy employed.

The first and most significant limitation is the use of **non-probability convenience sampling**. Because participants were recruited through personal contacts and institutional networks rather than drawn from a population registry by probabilistic means, the sample may overrepresent nurses employed at facilities with more advanced digital infrastructure or with a particularly engaged head nurse willing to facilitate data collection. The impact of this bias was partially mitigated by deliberately including facilities from at least three Slovak regions and by achieving representation from both inpatient and outpatient settings. Nevertheless, findings cannot be generalised to the entire Slovak nursing workforce without explicit qualification, and should be interpreted as indicative rather than definitive.

The second limitation concerns **self-report measurement**. All data in this study — including the frequency of digital tool use and self-assessments of work efficiency — derive from respondents' subjective reports rather than from objective usage logs, administrative records, or independently observed performance indicators. Self-report data are susceptible to recall bias, to acquiescence bias, and to the social desirability effects described in the preceding section. Anonymity and neutral item wording were employed as mitigations, but cannot entirely eliminate subjective reporting distortions.

The third limitation is the **cross-sectional design**. Because all data were collected at a single time point, the study cannot establish the temporal sequence or directionality of the associations examined. The observed correlations between education or experience and technology-related variables do not permit causal inference and should not be interpreted as indicating that educational attainment produces higher technology use. All interpretive language in this thesis is accordingly restricted to associational and descriptive claims.

The fourth limitation is the **absence of test-retest reliability assessment** for the self-constructed instrument. The temporal stability of the questionnaire — that is, the extent to which it yields consistent responses from the same individuals across administrations separated by a short interval — was not evaluated, due to logistical constraints associated with recruiting the same participants twice within the available timeline. Internal consistency was assessed as a partial substitute; future research employing this instrument or a derivative should include a test-retest component with a two- to four-week interval.

The fifth limitation is the **non-standardised character of the research instrument**. Unlike validated scales with established psychometric histories, the questionnaire constructed for this study has been subjected to expert review and pilot testing but has not undergone formal multi-stage validation with independent samples. The observed internal consistency coefficients therefore represent preliminary evidence of reliability rather than established psychometric quality. Future research should pursue formal validation of the instrument, including factor analysis with an adequately sized independent sample.

The sixth limitation is the **restriction of the study to the Slovak context**. The findings reflect the specific digital infrastructure, nursing education system, healthcare organisational structures, and regulatory environment of Slovakia and may not transfer directly to other national settings. Cross-national comparisons should be made cautiously and only with reference to contextual similarities.
