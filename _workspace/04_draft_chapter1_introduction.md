# Chapter 1: Introduction

## 1.1 Research Background

The global healthcare landscape has undergone a profound transformation over the past two decades, driven by the accelerating integration of digital technologies into clinical practice. Electronic health records (EHR), telemedicine platforms, mobile health applications, clinical decision-support systems, and smart patient monitoring devices have collectively reshaped the organisation of care delivery, the structure of clinical documentation, and the informational demands placed on healthcare professionals. Worldwide, the share of hospitals operating comprehensive EHR systems rose from below five percent in the early 2000s to more than eighty percent in leading healthcare economies by the mid-2020s (Ge et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2023). The European Union reinforced this trajectory with the adoption of Regulation (EU) 2025/327 establishing the European Health Data Space, which requires Member States to support priority data exchange by March 2029 (da Silva Carvalho et al., 2025). These developments signal not merely a technological trend but a structural reconfiguration of healthcare systems in which digital competence has become a prerequisite for safe and effective clinical practice.

Nursing, as the most numerous professional group within healthcare systems, occupies a central position in this digital transformation. Nurses are primary users of EHR systems, principal operatives of monitoring technologies, and front-line communicators through digital handover and care coordination platforms. The capacity of the nursing workforce to adopt, effectively use, and critically evaluate digital tools therefore has direct consequences for patient safety, care continuity, and institutional efficiency. The professional literature has responded accordingly: theoretical frameworks for understanding technology adoption in nursing — most notably the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) (Davis, 1989), its successor the Unified Theory of Acceptance and Use of Technology (UTAUT) (Venkatesh et al., 2003), and Rogers' (2003) Diffusion of Innovations theory — have been applied extensively to nursing populations, yielding a robust body of evidence on the predictors of nurses' technology acceptance and the organisational conditions under which digital tools translate into improved clinical outcomes.

Within this international context, the Slovak healthcare system has pursued its own digital transformation trajectory. The national eHealth platform — eZdravie — launched on 1 January 2018, introducing the Electronic Health Booklet (Elektronická zdravotná knižka, EZK), the electronic prescription (ePrescription), eExamination records, and a Patient Summary accessible to all registered healthcare providers (Štempeľová, Hudáková & Takáč, 2023). This represented a landmark institutional shift, embedding digital documentation requirements into the legal obligations of healthcare facilities and professionals. The legislative basis — the Slovak eHealth Act (Act No. 153/2013 Coll.) and its subsequent amendments — created a framework that formally integrates nursing documentation into the national electronic health information infrastructure. Despite these legislative and technological advances, empirical evidence on how Slovak nurses actually interact with these systems in everyday clinical practice, and how they assess the impact of digital tools on their work and on patient care, remains largely absent from the published literature.

Healthcare systems across Central and Eastern Europe, including Slovakia, share structural characteristics that may render the adoption trajectories of Western European or North American contexts only partially applicable: an ageing nursing workforce, a nursing education system spanning secondary vocational and university-level programmes with varying informatics content, healthcare facilities at different stages of EMRAM (Electronic Medical Record Adoption Model) progression, and dual documentation burdens arising from the coexistence of paper-based and electronic records in many clinical environments (Bartosiewicz, Burzyńska & Januszewicz, 2021; Girasek, Döbrössy, Boros & Győrffy, 2024). Understanding how these structural conditions shape nurses' technology use and attitudes is essential for evidence-based policy in nursing education, workforce development, and health informatics.

## 1.2 Problem Statement

Despite the extensive international literature on digital technology adoption in nursing, a significant evidence gap persists with respect to the Slovak clinical nursing context. The frequency and breadth of digital tool use among Slovak nurses across both inpatient and outpatient settings have not been systematically measured. The relationship between nurses' educational credentials — a particularly relevant variable given the co-existence of secondary vocational, bachelor's, and master's-level nursing education pathways in Slovakia — and their patterns of technology adoption has not been empirically examined in a Slovak clinical sample. Similarly, the relationship between years of clinical experience and nurses' attitudes toward digital technologies, while theoretically predicted by both UTAUT and Diffusion of Innovations frameworks and empirically supported in several Central European studies (Bartosiewicz, Burzyňska & Januszewicz, 2021; Girasek et al., 2024), has not been investigated among Slovak nursing staff specifically. Finally, the proportion of Slovak nurses who perceive EHR systems as contributing to improved quality and safety of patient care — a clinically and policy-relevant indicator — is unknown.

This knowledge gap has practical consequences. Without empirical data on the current state of digital tool adoption in Slovak nursing, healthcare institutions, nursing educators, and healthcare IT policymakers lack the evidentiary basis for targeted interventions. Decisions regarding nursing informatics curricula, continuing professional development in digital competencies, and investment priorities for clinical IT infrastructure are currently made without the foundation of nationally grounded survey evidence. The international literature, while informative, is not a substitute: evidence from the United States, Australia, or even neighbouring Poland or Hungary reflects healthcare system structures, IT deployment patterns, and nursing education systems that differ materially from the Slovak context. A study grounded in the Slovak clinical environment is therefore not a replication of existing research but an original contribution to a genuinely underexplored empirical field.

## 1.3 Theoretical Basis

The present study draws on three interconnected theoretical frameworks that together provide the conceptual scaffolding for both the research design and the interpretation of findings.

The Technology Acceptance Model (TAM), originally proposed by Davis (1989), holds that an individual's adoption of an information technology is primarily determined by two perceptual constructs: perceived usefulness — the degree to which the individual believes the technology will enhance job performance — and perceived ease of use — the degree to which the technology is believed to require minimal effort. These perceptions shape attitudes toward use, which in turn drive behavioural intention and actual usage. TAM has been applied to nursing populations in numerous cross-sectional surveys (Xie, Zhang & Zhu, 2025; Walzer et al., 2025) and remains the most cited individual-level framework in nursing informatics research. In the context of the present study, perceived usefulness corresponds to the construct of self-rated work efficiency assessed in Hypothesis 1, while the determinants of perceived ease of use are partially captured by nurses' educational background (Hypothesis 2) and accumulated clinical experience (Hypothesis 3).

The Unified Theory of Acceptance and Use of Technology (UTAUT), developed by Venkatesh, Morris, Davis and Davis (2003), extends TAM by integrating four core determinants of adoption intention — performance expectancy, effort expectancy, social influence, and facilitating conditions — and by explicitly incorporating moderating roles for age, gender, experience, and voluntariness of use. The explicit inclusion of experience as a moderating variable renders UTAUT particularly apposite for the investigation of H3, which concerns the relationship between years of clinical practice and nurses' attitudinal orientation toward digital technologies. Multiple studies in nursing informatics have found that effort expectancy carries greater weight among older and more experienced workers, whose accumulated non-digital work routines create higher switching costs when new technologies are introduced (Han, Kim, Ryu & Shin, 2024; Bartosiewicz, Burzyńska & Januszewicz, 2021).

Rogers' (2003) Diffusion of Innovations theory provides a complementary macro-level perspective by framing technology adoption as a process distributed across a social system over time. Rogers classifies adopters into five categories — innovators, early adopters, early majority, late majority, and laggards — whose proportional representation and sequential engagement with a technology determine the overall adoption rate. Crucially, Rogers identifies five attributes of an innovation that affect its rate of diffusion: relative advantage, compatibility with existing values and practices, complexity, trialability, and observability. Applied to the Slovak nursing context, Diffusion of Innovations explains why EHR uptake may vary across clinical departments and institutional settings, and provides a theoretical basis for understanding the distribution of technology use frequencies observed in the survey data. The model also contextualises the critical role of peer champions — nurses who serve as "superusers" or opinion leaders within their units — in accelerating technology diffusion beyond early adopters (Corbett et al., 2025).

Together, these three frameworks generate an integrated conceptual model in which individual nurses' perceptions of digital tools (TAM), moderated by their educational credentials and clinical experience (UTAUT), determine adoption behaviours that diffuse through institutional and professional social systems at rates shaped by the perceived characteristics of the tools themselves (Diffusion of Innovations). This multi-level theoretical architecture provides the interpretive lens through which both descriptive findings and hypothesis test results are analysed in the subsequent chapters.

## 1.4 Research Gap

The published literature on digital technology adoption in nursing is extensive but geographically and contextually uneven. Systematic reviews and umbrella reviews (Walzer et al., 2025; Nascimento et al., 2023) aggregate evidence predominantly from high-income English-speaking countries, Scandinavian healthcare systems, and East Asian contexts. Central European countries — Slovakia, Czechia, Hungary, Poland — are represented sparsely, and in the cases where they do appear, the research focuses predominantly on physician rather than nursing populations (Girasek et al., 2024) or on nursing student rather than practising clinical nurse samples (Pajari et al., 2025). The sole major Slovak-adjacent study identified through systematic search — the cross-sectional survey by Bartosiewicz, Burzyńska and Januszewicz (2021) of 849 nurses in southeastern Poland — provides valuable CEE-region data but cannot be assumed to reflect the Slovak institutional context directly, given Poland's different IT infrastructure trajectory, different nursing education system structure, and different eHealth regulatory environment.

Within Slovakia, no peer-reviewed cross-sectional survey of clinical nurses' digital tool use frequency, technology attitudes, or perceived impact of digital tools on care quality and efficiency was identified in any of the eight systematic search processes conducted for this thesis. The available Slovak-language publications on eHealth are predominantly policy documents, institutional reports, or descriptive overviews of the eZdravie infrastructure, rather than empirical studies of clinical staff behaviour. This constitutes a genuine and substantial research gap that the present study is positioned to address.

The gap is further sharpened by the particular structure of the Slovak nursing workforce. Slovakia's nursing education system produces graduates at two principal qualification levels — secondary vocational (maturita) and higher education (Bc., Mgr.) — whose curricula may differ substantially in informatics content. UTAUT and TAM-based research in other countries suggests that educational level is a significant predictor of both digital competency and frequency of technology use (Hariyati et al., 2024; Comparcini et al., 2025; Pajari et al., 2025); whether this relationship holds in the Slovak clinical nursing population has not been empirically tested. Similarly, the Slovak nursing workforce is ageing — a trend shared across Central Europe — and the relationship between clinical seniority and technology attitudes has significant implications for workforce planning and continuing professional development priorities that remain empirically uncharted in the Slovak context.

## 1.5 Research Purpose and Questions

The primary purpose of this study is to analyse the use of modern technologies and digital tools in nursing practice in Slovakia, with a focus on the frequency and types of tools used in clinical settings, nurses' perceived impact of these tools on work efficiency and patient care quality, and the relationship between nurses' professional and educational characteristics and their technology-related attitudes and behaviours.

This primary goal is operationalised through five partial research goals. The first is to determine the frequency and types of digital tools used by nurses in their daily clinical practice across inpatient and outpatient settings. The second is to assess nurses' self-rated perception of the impact of digital tools on their personal work efficiency. The third is to examine nurses' attitudes toward digital technology implementation in healthcare facilities. The fourth is to determine the impact of digital tools on work efficiency, communication, and quality of care as perceived by nursing staff. The fifth is to assess nurses' evaluation of the impact of modern technologies on the quality, safety, and continuity of nursing care.

Four research hypotheses guide the inferential component of the study and were formulated on the basis of the theoretical frameworks and empirical literature reviewed above.

Hypothesis 1 (H1) states that there is a statistically significant relationship between the frequency of digital tool use and nurses' self-rated work efficiency. This hypothesis is grounded in the TAM construct of perceived usefulness and in the empirical finding that nurses who engage with digital tools more frequently tend to report higher perceived efficiency gains, suggesting that familiarity and habituation mediate the relationship between tool use and efficiency appraisal (Xie, Zhang & Zhu, 2025; Upadhyay & Hu, 2022).

Hypothesis 2 (H2) states that nurses with higher education — at bachelor's degree level or above — use modern technologies more frequently than nurses who completed secondary vocational nursing education. This hypothesis is theoretically grounded in the TIGER nursing informatics competency framework, the DigComp framework, and Human Capital Theory, and is empirically supported by evidence from multiple cross-sectional studies demonstrating a positive association between formal educational credential level and both digital competency scores and frequency of digital tool use in clinical practice (Hariyati et al., 2024; Comparcini et al., 2025; Pajari et al., 2025).

Hypothesis 3 (H3) states that there is a statistically significant relationship between years of clinical experience and attitude toward digital technologies in nursing practice. This hypothesis reflects the UTAUT prediction that effort expectancy — the perceived cognitive and physical effort required to use a technology — carries increasing weight as a barrier to adoption among more experienced workers whose established clinical routines represent higher switching costs. The Polish cross-sectional study by Bartosiewicz, Burzyňska and Januszewicz (2021) and the Hungarian physician survey by Girasek et al. (2024) both confirm age and years of practice as significant predictors of digital health attitudes in CEE healthcare populations, providing the regional empirical basis for this hypothesis.

Hypothesis 4 (H4) states that more than fifty percent of nurses rate EHR systems as contributing to improved quality and safety of patient care. This directional proportion hypothesis is informed by systematic review evidence demonstrating that EHR-mediated clinical decision support and electronic medication management systems are associated with reductions of up to fifty-four percent in medication errors and substantial improvements in care coordination and patient safety outcomes (Insani et al., 2025; Syrowatka et al., 2024; Lazzari, 2024). The hypothesis tests whether Slovak nurses' subjective assessments align with the objective outcome evidence documented in the international literature.

## 1.6 Thesis Organisation

This thesis is organised into six chapters, each addressing a distinct phase of the research process while contributing to the overarching analytical argument.

Chapter 1, the present chapter, situates the study within the global and Slovak contexts of healthcare digitalisation, identifies the research gap it addresses, presents the theoretical framework guiding the investigation, and formally states the research goals and hypotheses.

Chapter 2 provides the theoretical and empirical background. It elaborates the conceptual definitions of key terms — digital health, health informatics, nursing informatics, eHealth, and mHealth — and reviews the international literature on the principal digital tool categories relevant to nursing practice: electronic health records, telemedicine, mobile health applications, and smart monitoring systems. The chapter then synthesises empirical evidence on nurses' attitudes toward digital technologies and on the impact of digitalisation on care quality, safety, and continuity, and closes with a presentation of the research model and hypotheses as derived from this literature.

Chapter 3 describes the research methodology in detail. It specifies the quantitative, non-experimental, cross-sectional survey design adopted for the study; the composition and derivation of the research sample; the construction, content validity assessment, and pilot testing of the non-standardised research questionnaire; the procedures for data collection across inpatient and outpatient settings; and the statistical methods applied in data analysis, including descriptive statistics, the Pearson Chi-square test of independence, and the one-sample z-test for proportions.

Chapter 4 presents the results of the empirical investigation. Findings are reported objectively through descriptive statistics, frequency distributions, cross-tabulations, and the outcomes of inferential tests for each of the four hypotheses, supported by tables and graphical displays. Chapter 4 contains no interpretation of findings; this is reserved for the subsequent chapter.

Chapter 5 provides a discussion of the results in relation to the theoretical framework and the prior empirical literature. Each hypothesis is evaluated in light of the statistical evidence obtained, and the findings are compared systematically with analogous studies from the international and Central European literature. The chapter addresses the theoretical and practical implications of the results, proposes concrete recommendations for nursing practice, nursing education, and healthcare policy in Slovakia, and identifies the study's methodological limitations alongside directions for future research.

Chapter 6 concludes the thesis by summarising the extent to which the stated research goals were achieved and by situating the study's contribution within the broader context of nursing informatics and healthcare digitalisation in Slovakia.

The thesis closes with a complete list of references formatted in accordance with the ISO 690 standard, and with appendices containing the full research questionnaire and any supplementary data tables.
