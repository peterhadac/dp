# Chapter 6: Conclusion

## 6.1 Summary of Theoretical Work Accomplished

The present thesis was conceived in response to a demonstrable and consequential gap in the Slovak nursing literature: the relative absence of systematic empirical evidence concerning the extent, nature, and perceived impact of digital technology use among practising nurses. In addressing this gap, the work undertaken in the theoretical part of the thesis pursued two interrelated objectives — to establish a rigorous conceptual and empirical foundation upon which the subsequent empirical investigation could be built, and to situate the Slovak nursing context within the broader international discourse on healthcare digitalisation.

The theoretical chapters traced the development and current state of digital technology integration in nursing practice, drawing on international literature from health informatics, nursing science, and the sociology of professional knowledge. The evolution of electronic health record systems from early paper-based documentation practices toward integrated, interoperable digital platforms was examined, as was the expanding role of clinical decision-support tools, telemedicine systems, mobile health applications, and diagnostically relevant connected devices in daily nursing work. The theoretical analysis demonstrated that these technologies, when implemented under appropriate conditions and supported by adequate training structures, have been associated with measurable improvements in documentation accuracy, reduction in medication errors, enhancement of interprofessional communication, and greater continuity of patient care across care transitions.

Particular attention was paid to the conceptual frameworks that have informed research on technology acceptance and adoption in healthcare settings. The Technology Acceptance Model and its subsequent extensions provided a theoretically grounded basis for understanding why individual nurses may differ in their orientation toward digital tools — differences that appear to be systematically related to educational background, clinical experience, and the organisational environment in which nurses practice. These insights informed the formulation of the four research hypotheses that structure the empirical component of this study.

The theoretical work also examined the Slovak healthcare context specifically. The progressive introduction of national electronic health record infrastructure, regulatory mandates for digital documentation, and the ongoing transformation of nursing education in Slovakia were documented and analysed. This contextualisation is essential: findings from international studies conducted in healthcare systems with longer-established digital infrastructure and different professional training traditions cannot be uncritically transposed to the Slovak setting. An evidence base grounded in the specific conditions of Slovak nursing practice is therefore not merely academically valuable — it is a prerequisite for any policy or curriculum interventions aimed at optimising technology integration in this context.

## 6.2 Design and Validation of the Research Instrument

A significant contribution of the theoretical part of this thesis lies in the development of a purpose-built, non-standardised research questionnaire designed to measure digital technology use and its perceived impacts among Slovak nurses. No existing validated instrument was identified in the reviewed literature that adequately covered the full range of digital tools deployed in contemporary Slovak inpatient and outpatient nursing practice while also being available in the Slovak language and calibrated to the educational and organisational characteristics of the Slovak nursing workforce.

The instrument was constructed through a systematic four-stage process: conceptual mapping of relevant constructs derived from the literature review; generation of an initial item pool; content validity review conducted by subject-matter experts; and pilot testing with nurses drawn from the target population. This process yielded a 36-item questionnaire structured into four thematic sections addressing sociodemographic and professional characteristics, patterns of digital tool use, perceived impact on work efficiency, and attitudes toward digitalisation and electronic health record systems. Pilot testing confirmed the intelligibility of items and the practical feasibility of the instrument for self-administration within a clinical setting.

The development of this instrument represents a methodological contribution in its own right. The questionnaire constitutes a reusable research tool that may be employed by subsequent investigators studying digital technology integration in Slovak or comparable Central European nursing contexts, subject to further formal psychometric validation.

## 6.3 Research Questions Formulated for the Empirical Phase

The theoretical work established a clear and operationally precise agenda for the empirical investigation that follows data collection. Four research hypotheses were formulated and their key variables operationally defined. The first hypothesis examines whether a statistically significant relationship exists between the frequency of digital tool use and nurses' subjective assessment of their own work efficiency — a relationship suggested by the international literature but not yet empirically tested in the Slovak context. The second hypothesis addresses whether nurses with completed higher education use digital technologies more frequently than nurses whose highest qualification is at the secondary vocational level, a question with direct implications for nursing education policy. The third hypothesis investigates whether years of clinical experience are associated with attitudinal orientation toward digital technologies, which is theoretically relevant because both technophilic and technophobic orientations have been reported in experienced clinical populations in other national studies. The fourth hypothesis tests the directional claim that a majority of Slovak nurses — specifically more than fifty percent — assess electronic health record systems as contributing to improvements in the quality and safety of patient care.

These four hypotheses were not selected arbitrarily. They were derived systematically from the intersection of theoretically identified constructs, identified evidence gaps in the existing Slovak-language literature, and the practical informational needs of nursing educators, healthcare managers, and digital health policy-makers operating within the Slovak system.

## 6.4 Significance and Expected Contribution

The significance of this study is rooted in the particular historical moment at which Slovak healthcare finds itself. Healthcare institutions across Slovakia are engaged in progressively deeper integration of digital infrastructure, yet the voice of nursing — the professional group most directly and continuously engaged with the patient, and therefore most directly affected by changes in documentation, communication, and monitoring technologies — has been largely absent from the empirical literature on this transformation. The present study was designed to begin addressing that absence.

If the empirical phase yields data consistent with the directional predictions embedded in the four hypotheses, the findings will provide evidence supporting investment in digital skills training particularly for nurses at the secondary vocational education level, suggest that experience-based professional socialization shapes but does not uniformly determine attitudes toward technology, and indicate whether the current generation of Slovak nurses broadly endorses or qualifies the positive claims made for electronic health record systems in the international literature. If the data disconfirm these hypotheses, the findings will be equally informative — directing attention toward alternative explanatory mechanisms and cautioning against the uncritical adoption of assumptions generated in non-Slovak healthcare environments.

Beyond the immediate hypotheses, the descriptive findings of the survey will document the landscape of digital tool use in Slovak nursing practice at a specific historical moment, providing a baseline against which future studies can measure change. This longitudinal potential is among the more enduring contributions the study may offer.

## 6.5 Toward the Empirical Phase

The theoretical part of this thesis fulfilled the goals assigned to it. A comprehensive review of the relevant international and Slovak literature was completed; the key theoretical constructs were defined and their interrelationships articulated; the research problem was precisely stated; research goals and hypotheses were formulated with operational definitions suitable for statistical testing; and a purpose-built research instrument was developed, expert-reviewed, and pilot-tested. The methodological design — a quantitative, non-experimental, descriptive-analytical cross-sectional survey targeting a sample of 150 to 200 registered nurses employed in inpatient and outpatient settings across Slovakia — was specified in sufficient detail to ensure replicability.

What remains is the empirical phase: the distribution and collection of completed questionnaires, the entry and verification of data, statistical analysis using the techniques specified in Chapter 3, and the interpretation of findings in light of the theoretical framework established in the preceding chapters. The results, presented in Chapter 4, and the discussion offered in Chapter 5, will determine the extent to which the hypotheses formulated on the basis of the literature are supported by evidence drawn from the Slovak nursing workforce, and will articulate the implications of those findings for nursing practice, education, and healthcare policy.

The theoretical work accomplished here has laid the necessary groundwork. The question of what Slovak nurses actually do with digital technologies, what they think about them, and how those orientations relate to their educational and experiential backgrounds now awaits an empirical answer.
